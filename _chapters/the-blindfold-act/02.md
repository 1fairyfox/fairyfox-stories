title: A Cold Reading

A cold reading is what we call it when you know nothing and must seem to know everything. You have no
file on the mark, no plant in the tip, no confederate whispering through the canvas. You have a
stranger sitting down in front of you and thirty seconds, and you build them out of their shoes.

It is the hardest thing in the trade and the only thing I am proud of. Everybody can do a hot reading.
Pay the ticket-seller a nickel to listen at the line and you can tell a woman her sister's name and make
her cry. That is not a skill, that is a purchase. Cold is different. Cold is looking at what is actually
in front of you, honestly, with no story of your own in the way, and saying the true thing before you
have decided whether you want it to be true.

I have made my living at cold readings for fifteen years. I want that on the record now, at the start,
because of what happened in the third week, and because a woman who is good at a thing and then does not
do it has to be asked why, and I would rather be the one asking.

The sheriff took Bruno at nine in the morning, in front of everybody, which was the point of doing it at
nine in the morning.

Not arrested. He was careful about that, our sheriff, he had that much law in him. He *walked him over*
to the county car with a hand at his elbow like an usher, and stood him against the fender in the sun
where the whole lot could see, and asked him questions we could all hear the shape of if not the words,
and the shape was: *those hands*. Bruno has hands like a pair of hams gone to college. He lifts a
hollow dumbbell for the rubes and bends a bar that has been bent nine hundred times before, and there is
not one honest ounce of strength in the whole act, and it does not matter, because a town looks at a man
that size and does the arithmetic in a second and a half and never checks its work.

He answered everything. That was the awful part. I stood at the back of the ten-in-one with a cup of
cook-house coffee going cold in my hand and I watched a man from Toledo answer a tired sheriff
truthfully, at length, with his hat in his enormous fingers, and I watched the sheriff write down
nothing at all, because he was not gathering; he was *fitting*. I know the look. It is the look I wear
behind a blindfold when a mark has already told me what they came to hear and I am only waiting for a
polite place to say it back to them.

Then the patch came out of the cook-house tent with his coat on and his voice already warm, and I
watched the second half of the machine start.

Our patch is a small pink man named Dell who has never in his life said a sentence he could not take
back, and he is very good, and I have loved him the way you love a lock on your own door. He went to
that sheriff sideways, at an angle of pure respect, and he did the thing he does, which is to make a
lawman feel like the only sensible person in a hundred miles. And I could not hear him but I could read
him at forty feet with my eyes half shut, because Dell's whole body says the same three sentences every
time and they are: *this is terrible, we are cooperating, we will be gone by Friday.*

He was not selling Bruno. I want to be fair, and I have earned the right to be fair about Dell because I
was not fair to him later. Dell was not standing there saying *take the strongman*. Dell was standing
there saying *the show is clean and the show is leaving*, and the sheriff was standing there needing a
name, and when a man who needs a name is told very warmly that the name is not in *here*, he does not
stop needing a name. He looks at the man against the fender who is not quite *in here* either. Hired
muscle from Toledo. One season with us. No wall, no gaff, no road to run to. Not with it.

Nobody sold Bruno. We simply all stood in a circle and left him outside it, which is the same, and
costs less, and lets you sleep.

That is the commandment I told you about, and I want to show you the underside of it, since I have
spent my life underneath. *With it and for it.* It is the best thing about us. It is why a runaway
with a bruise she won't name can walk onto a mud lot and be fed by nightfall and taught a trade by
spring and never once asked a question she doesn't want to answer. It is a real love. There is no
counterfeit in it.

It is also a wall, and a wall does not know what it is keeping out. It keeps out the sheriff and the
county and the church ladies, and it keeps out Bruno from Toledo, and it keeps out anybody's plain
question about a padlock, and it keeps out — but I am ahead of myself again. Every good thing about us
was, that week, a machine for not looking. And I was the finest instrument the machine had ever
grown, and it had grown me on purpose, and I had let it, because being the eye of a thing that refuses
to see is a very comfortable place to hide.

So. The circle.

I did it at the cook-house at noon, which is where a show tells itself the truth if it is ever going
to, and I did it the way I would work a tip: nobody knew they were sitting down at my table.

Sallow the cook found the body, and Sallow was not sleeping, and Sallow's hands shook on the ladle in a
way that I read in one pass as exactly what it looked like — a sixty-year-old man who had put his boot
through a window and climbed into the dark and touched the old man's shoulder to wake him. Some things
are what they look like. Cleared, and I felt the small clean pleasure of it, and did not yet notice
that I had begun the list with the easiest face on the lot, the way a coward eats the crust first.

Perro the fire-eater sat with his back to the tent wall and did not eat, and read a letter he had
already read, and put it in the inside pocket over his heart where a man puts a thing that frightens
him, and I have known for two months that Perro owes money in three towns to the sort of man who does
not write twice. He wanted the Colonel to pay it. He had asked the Colonel to pay it. The Colonel, who
believed a father is owed, had said what fathers say. So: a man in debt, in the show, on the lot, on the
night the week's take walked. Write it down. Everybody would.

Ynez the fortune-teller crossed herself, which she does for weather, and told four people how she had
felt it coming and how she had *said* so, and I read her the way you read a woman who has spent nine
years being the second-best mystic on a lot with only two. She hated him. She said so freely, which is
the tell that it is nothing; the hate people hand you across a table is never the hate that kills. She
had also been at cards in the cook-house tent until the sky went grey, with three witnesses who did not
love her enough to lie. Write her down and cross her out. I did both. It took me eleven minutes and I
was, God help me, *enjoying myself* — the old hot pleasure of the work, the tumblers going over, a
whole world coming apart into tells.

And then Dell came in and stood in the doorway of the tent and said, to nobody, in the flat voice under
his warm one: "Well. He'd want us to roll."

Meaning the Colonel. Meaning: the old man would have wanted the show to go on, and would have wanted it
in exactly those words, and every one of us knew it and half of us said *he would* out loud, and it was
the first time his name had been in the air since the mud.

And in the corner of my eye, three feet from my elbow, Lark's hands stopped.

I want to be honest about how small it was, because if I make it a thunderclap you will not believe the
rest. She was cutting bread. Sixteen, or says so, half a step out of every huddle she has ever stood in,
cutting bread for a family she has been part of for one year and eleven days. And when Dell said *he'd
want us to roll*, the knife stopped, and her thumb came up off the loaf, and she looked at the cold
stove at the end of the tent — not at Dell, not at the door, at the *stove* — for one second and a
half, and then went back to the bread with her face on perfectly straight.

Nobody in that tent saw it. There was no one in the world in that tent who could have seen it but me.

That is not pride. That is the charge. Understand me: I am the only person alive who was equipped to
notice, and I noticed, and my noticing is not something I can turn off any more than you can unhear
your own name. The cold clerk at the back of me had the whole thing before my coffee got to my mouth:
*the stove, the padlock, the spare-hasp turn I teach a girl her first week, and grief that is arranged
instead of arriving.*

And the rest of me — the fifteen-years-behind-a-blindfold rest of me — did what it has always done. It
said: she is a child who has lost the only roof she has, and the stove is where they found him, and of
course the poor thing looks at the stove, and you are a sick woman, Ada Prewitt, who reads guilt into a
girl cutting bread because reading is the only thing you have instead of a life.

Both of those were true. That is the trouble with a good reading. It does not come with a receipt.

She came to my wagon that night.

I had the lamp low and the blindfold on the table where I put it, and I was doing what I always do
after a bad day, which is nothing, in a very orderly way. She stood in the door with her elbows in her
hands. She had been outside it for a while; I had heard the boards.

"Sonora," she said. "If a person —"

And I want you to sit with that for a second, because I have sat with it for the rest of my life. Six
words. The door of her, standing open. A girl of sixteen in a canvas doorway with her elbows in her
hands, starting a sentence that had *if a person* at the front of it, which is the oldest way in the
world to say *I*.

I knew what it was. Do not let me off. I did not suspect, I did not wonder, I *knew*, the way I know a
widow from a wife at nine feet — that whatever came after those six words was the true thing, the whole
thing, handed to me freely by the only person on that lot who had ever tried to hand me anything.

And I was so frightened that I did the act.

I did not decide to. That is the part I would like to lie to you about and won't. It came up out of me
smooth and warm and expert, the way it comes up in front of a tip, and it took her sentence off her
gently and put a lesson in her hands instead. I said — and I remember every word, they are cut into me
— I said: *"Don't stand in a doorway, child, it reads guilty. Come in or go. And whatever you're about
to tell me: don't. Not tonight, not to me, not to anybody. That's the trade. The tip pays for the
mystery, and the mystery is the part you never say out loud. You keep your own gaff, Lark. That's the
whole wall, and I built you a good one, and I'd hate to watch you knock a brick out of it over
nothing."*

Warm. Kind. Perfectly delivered. The best cold reading of my life, on a girl I have never once read
wrong, and every word of it a hand on her chest pushing her back out into the dark.

I watched the wall go up behind her eyes. I had built it; I knew every course of brick in it; I
watched it come up to full height in about two seconds, and it was beautiful work, and it was mine.
She said, "No. It's nothing." She said it in my voice. Then she said goodnight, and thanked me, and
went out, and the lot was very quiet, and I sat there in a canvas wagon with a spangled blindfold on the
table in front of me and my heart going like a hammer.

I could have called her back. She was six feet away. She was six feet away for about a minute and I
counted the whole minute and did not open my mouth, and after that she was not.

Here is the reading, then, for free, cold, no dime, and it is the only one I ever got wrong side out:
There was one person on that mud lot who could see through anything, and one person in that doorway
trying to be seen, and they were standing close enough to touch, and the thing that stopped it was not
the sheriff and not the code and not the Colonel dead in his box.

It was me. I did it. I did it *well*.

And I would spend the next four days doing brilliant, exhausting, expensive detective work to find out
a thing I had been offered for nothing in a doorway, and I would find it, and by the time I did it would
cost her everything, and I would still call myself the one who sees.
