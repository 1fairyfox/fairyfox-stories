title: The Weight of a True Map

I did not go down to Deck Nought.

Vosse had given me a month and the key, and I spent the first eleven days of that month doing my job
with a thoroughness that would have looked, to anyone watching, like devotion. I resurveyed Six-spinward,
which had not been walked in nineteen years. I found a coolant gate mislabelled since 388 and filed the
correction. I checked my own trailing lines twice and then a third time. I carried the key in the inner
pocket of my coat, against my ribs, and I did not take it out, and every morning I checked with two
fingers that it was still there, the way you check a wound.

I have thought a great deal, since, about why I worked so hard that month. The honest answer is that
measuring is the only thing that has ever made the world hold still for me. My mother used to say I
counted in my sleep. If a corridor is one hundred and sixty-nine point one one metres, then it is that,
and it will be that tomorrow, and nothing anybody says at a founding-day assembly can make it one
hundred and sixty-one. I had built a whole self out of that. In the eleven days after Chart Room 2 I
went out and measured things every waking hour, the way a man with a cracked bone will keep testing it
to see whether it still holds.

On the twelfth day the roster sent me to Nine-spinward, residential, sixty compartments, thirty-one
years since the last confirm.

---

You do not survey a residential corridor the way you survey a coolant run. There is a protocol, and
the protocol is mostly manners. You knock. You show the plate. You ask the head of the household for
leave to measure their doorway, because their doorway is their doorway and it is also a line on the
master chart and both of those things are true. And then you accept the tea, because a resurvey comes
around perhaps twice in a lifetime and it is an occasion, and to decline is an insult that will be
recounted for thirty years.

I had done it a hundred times. I liked it. It is the only part of the trade where the map has a face.

The Tolvers had compartment forty-one. Renk Tolver worked the vats and had the grey hands you get from
the vats; Mira Tolver mended and traded and did the corridor's accounts in her head faster than I could
do them on a plate. There was a baby, unnamed yet — naming comes at four months on the *Meridian*, and
she was three — and there was a boy of nine called Emil, and there was Renk's mother, Bettine, who was
eighty-one and dying, in the lower bunk, with the curtain hooked back so she could see the door.

They gave me tea. They gave me the good tea, which they could not afford, and I drank it, because to
refuse a thing a person cannot afford to give you is not humility, it is theft.

Then Emil Tolver, who had been vibrating in the corner for four minutes like a strut in a bad harmonic,
asked whether I was a *real* cartographer, and when I said yes he ran to the locker and came back with
a piece of stock the size of my hand, and put it in front of me, and stood there with his fists at his
sides waiting for judgement.

It was the drift diagram.

He had copied it out of the schoolroom sheet. The near lights and the far lights, the little arrows,
the ship as a fat sliver in the middle. He had done it in a hand that was much better than a nine-year-old's
hand has any right to be, and he had ruled the arrows with something straight, and he had lettered the
caption underneath in careful capitals, from memory, wrong in one place and heartbreaking in all of them:

*THIS IS HOW WE KNOW WE ARE GOING SOMEWHERE.*

I drew that sheet. In 409, for the schools. It took me two weeks. I chose the projection and I chose the
line weights and I remember being pleased with the arrows.

"That's a clean copy," I said. My voice did what I told it to. "You laid the arrows in properly. Most
people fudge the arrows."

He went red to the ears and looked at the floor and was, for a few seconds, unable to speak, and Mira
Tolver looked at her son with an expression I have not got the equipment to describe, and Renk said,
gruffly, that the boy had it in mind to sit for the Office, and did I think — and stopped, because you
do not ask a thing like that outright.

"He should sit," I said. "Tell them Renn sent him."

---

I measured the doorway. Two point one one metres, and the chart said two point one one, and I wrote
*confirmed* in the remarks field, and the letters went down as steady as they have ever gone down.

And then Emil showed me the frame.

I had seen the marks on other frames — everyone has; there is not a residential doorway on this ship
without something cut into it — but I had never once looked at what the marks *said*, because a doorway
is a line on a chart and what people scratch on their own property is not the Office's business. He put
his thumb on the top notch and started at the top and I could not stop him and I did not want to.

It is called the reckoning. I did not know that. Nine-spinward has kept it since the third generation
and half the ship keeps some version of it and nobody has ever thought to tell a cartographer, because
what would a cartographer want with it.

One mark for each child of the household, cut on their naming-day. And beside each mark, in small
figures, the distance.

The distance is read out at the naming. It comes from the founding-day proclamation, which gives the
ship's cumulative run to date, and the head of the household copies the figure off the corridor board
and cuts it into the frame beside the new child's mark, so that the child grows up beside a number, and
the number is the ground the child stands on: *this is how far the world had come when you began.*

There were nineteen marks on the Tolvers' frame. The oldest was faint and the figures beside it were
worn nearly out. Emil's own was chest-high on him and the figures were sharp.

"There'll be one for her next month," he said, meaning the baby, "and I'm allowed to cut it, because I'm
nine, and Da says my hand's steadier than his." He looked up at me. He had his mother's directness and
his father's grey hands coming. "You'd know the number, though. Wouldn't you? The real one, not the
board — the board's a fortnight behind. You could tell me the *exact* one, for her."

Behind him, in the lower bunk, Bettine Tolver turned her head on the pillow.

I had not thought she was awake. She had not spoken since I came in. She was eighty-one and she was
going, and everyone in that compartment knew it, and she lay there with the curtain hooked back and
looked at me out of a face that had gone down to its bones, and she said, in a voice like a dry page:

"And mine, cartographer. While you're at it."

"Ma," said Renk.

"Hush." She did not take her eyes off me. "Eighty-one years I've been aboard. I want to know what I
came." She got a breath. "How far did we get, in me?"

The compartment was very quiet. The vent ticked. Mira Tolver had stopped mending and had her hand over
her mouth, and Renk was looking at the deck, and Emil was looking at me with his fists at his sides
waiting for judgement, and the key was against my ribs where it had been for twelve days.

I want to be exact about what happened next, because I have been exact about everything else in this
account and I will not spare myself here.

I did not hesitate. That is the whole of it. I did not sit there struggling and then give way. There was
no struggle. Somebody asked me a question I have answered a thousand times and my mouth answered it,
and my hands came up onto the table and I did the arithmetic in front of them because that is what a
cartographer does, and I did it *well* — the cumulative run off the proclamation, the eighty-one-year
subtraction, carried out loud, the figure rounded honestly to three places because Bettine Tolver had
worked forty years in stores and would have known if I fudged it — and I turned the plate around and
showed her the number.

It is a large number. It is a beautiful number. It has been the same number, adjusted only for the
arithmetic of the story, for ninety-one years.

She looked at it for a long moment. Then she lay back, and her face — which had been a hard old face,
the face of a woman who had run a stores counter and buried a husband and did not care for fuss — her
face came apart, quietly, the way a face does when it is finally allowed to.

"That's all right, then," said Bettine Tolver. "That's a good bit of road."

And I said, "Yes. It is."

---

They wanted me to stay. I stayed two hours. I drank a second cup of the good tea and I told Emil the
exact figure for his sister's naming, corrected for the fortnight, and I wrote it on his drift diagram
in the corner in a proper cartographer's hand and initialled it, and he held it like a warrant. And at
the end Renk Tolver asked, awkwardly, hugely, whether — since I was here, since it was an occasion, since
the boy would remember it all his life — whether I would cut the mark.

So I did. I put the chisel in and I steadied the boy's hand over mine and we cut the nineteenth notch,
and then the twentieth, and then I laid the figures in beside it, small and square and true, in the good
steady line I have been able to draw since I was fourteen.

The hardest line in the trade is the one you have to draw for the thing you felt and not the thing you
can see. Vosse taught me that. I laid down that number in a doorway on Nine-spinward without a tremor.

---

I did not go back to the Office. I went to my bunk and I sat on it in my coat and I took the key out of
my inner pocket and put it on the shelf, and looked at it, and could not look at it, and turned it so
the worn edge faced the wall.

Eleven filings in the unlabelled folder. Eleven people in four hundred years who stood at the window and
came back up the corridor and drew the map to the wall. I had spent twelve days telling myself that
whatever else happened, I was not going to be the twelfth — that I would take the month, and think it
through, and decide, and that whatever I decided would at least be a *decision*, arrived at honestly, by
a person who counts.

I had not decided anything. Nobody had asked me to. Nobody had threatened me, or sworn me, or so much as
mentioned the Purpose.

I had simply been kind for four hours in a warm compartment on Nine-spinward, and drunk their good tea,
and been very good at it, and come home the twelfth.
