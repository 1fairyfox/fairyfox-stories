title: What the Founders Left

A person who counts cannot live beside a number she has refused to read. I had known that about myself
since I was fourteen, and I had spent thirteen days pretending it had stopped being true, and on the
fourteenth I took the key off the shelf and turned it right way round and went down to Deck Nought.

I did not tell Vosse. That was itself an answer, though I did not let myself hear it. Vosse had given me
the month and the key and had not once asked me what I meant to do, and I understand now that this was
not indifference but the exact opposite — that a person hands you a key without conditions only when they
have decided the choice must be wholly yours, and cannot be shared, and will not be lightened by their
opinion. Vosse had carried this for forty years. Vosse was tired. I did not see that either, on the
fourteenth day. I saw a corridor going down.

---

Deck Nought is cold in a way the rest of the ship is not, because the rest of the ship is kept warm for
people and Deck Nought is kept only from freezing, and there is a difference you feel in your teeth. I
had been down once, on the day I opened it, and had looked at the long-range instruments for as long as
it took to understand what they were telling me and then had come back up and not returned. This time I
made myself walk the whole length of it, all one hundred and forty metres, and measure it, because
measuring is the only prayer I have.

One hundred and forty point oh four metres. The charts say Deck Nought is solid hull. I have walked one
hundred and forty point oh four metres of solid hull. That is the sentence the whole ship is built on,
and I laid it down in my count and my hand did not shake, and I went to the observation blister at the
end where the founders had put the true instruments, and there, waiting, in a housing that had been made
to be found and not to be hidden — I want that distinction on the record, because it changed everything —
was the recording.

It was not concealed. It sat on a plain shelf below the long-range display in a reader old enough to be
in a museum, with a single plate beside it, and the plate did not say DO NOT or SEALED or any of the
words a secret wears. It said, in a hand I had been trained since childhood to recognise as the root of
my whole trade — the First Cartographer's hand, the hand the Office copies its capitals from to this day
— four words.

*For whoever measures true.*

I sat down on the cold deck in my coat and I played it.

---

She was old in the recording. I had not expected that. Every likeness of the First Cartographer shows her
at the founding, dark-haired, exact, a plate under her arm; and I had carried that picture so long that
the voice coming off the reader — a voice gone thin and unhurried, a voice with the founding a long way
behind it — undid me before she had said a full sentence, because it meant she had lived the whole way
into the lie and had made this at the end of it, knowing.

I will not give you all of it. Some of it was for me alone, in the way that a thing made for *whoever
measures true* is made for each person who ever does, one at a time, across four hundred years, and I
find I want to keep my share. But the spine of it I will set down, because it is the thing the Tolvers'
frame had already half taught me, and I would rather you had it from her.

She said: we did the arithmetic in the ninety-first year and the drive would not hold. She said: we
could have told you. We argued it for a year, the founders, in this room, and I argued for the truth,
because I was a cartographer and I believed then that a true map was a kindness in all weathers and a
false one a cruelty in all weathers, and I was wrong, and it took me the rest of my life to learn how I
was wrong, and I have made this so that you need not take as long.

She said: a people does not live on where it is going. A people lives on *believing it is going
somewhere.* Those are not the same, and I had drawn charts for sixty years before I understood that they
are not the same. Take away the destination and you do not get a people who face the truth bravely. You
get a people who stop cutting the number into the doorframe. You get a generation that does not name its
children after the distance, because the distance has stopped meaning anything, and then a generation
that does not name its children with much care at all. We had watched, in the models, what becomes of a
crew with no morning in it. We chose the morning. We chose to give you a horizon we knew was painted on
the inside of the hull, and to keep on painting it, generation after generation, in this office, with
these hands, because a painted horizon that a whole people stands up and walks toward is not nothing. It
is the most that was left to us. It is a road, if enough feet believe in it.

And then she said the thing I had come down to hear without knowing it, and she said it as one
cartographer to another, dry and level and without a grain of comfort in it:

*I could chart everything on this ship but this. Whether to tell you is the one line I could not draw
for you. It is not mine. It was never going to be mine. It belongs to whoever is holding the key when
the arithmetic will not reconcile any longer — and if you are hearing this, that is you, and I am sorry,
and I am not sorry, because someone has to hold it, and I would rather it were someone who measures
true.*

The recording ended. The reader ticked and went dark. Beyond the blister the long-range display held its
slow honest scatter of far lights, none of them getting nearer, all of them exactly where they had been
when I came down and would be when my own name was worn out of a doorframe somewhere on the spinward
side.

---

I sat a long time.

I had come down that corridor certain of one thing about myself, and I want to name it because it was
the thing the recording took away. I had believed that the truth and the kindness were, in the last
reckoning, the same object seen from two sides — that if you drew the map true and true and true, the
kindness would be in there somewhere, folded into the accuracy, and that anyone who set them against
each other was simply someone who had not yet measured carefully enough. It is a good faith to build a
trade on. It had held for me my whole life. It had held right up until a woman of eighty-one asked me
how far she had come, and I gave her a number I knew to be a story, and watched her face come apart with
something I have no better word for than *peace* — and it did not hold after that, and it was not going
to hold again, and the First Cartographer had known it would not, and had made a recording sixty years
long in the learning and four words long on the plate to meet me at the exact moment it broke.

Order or mercy. She had had the honesty not to pretend the map contained both.

The founders had chosen mercy, once, for everyone, forever, in this room — and had then done the one
thing that redeemed it, which was to refuse to make that choice binding. They had not sealed the deck.
They had not destroyed the instruments. They had left the true map lying in the cold at the bottom of a
corridor that measures longer than the hull it is drawn inside, with a plate that says *for whoever
measures true*, so that in every generation there would be one person free to walk down and undo the
whole kind lie with a single broadcast — and they had trusted that person, sight unseen, four hundred
years out, to weigh it.

That was the mercy that undid me, in the end. Not the painted horizon. The key.

---

I went back up. I did not broadcast anything; I want to be exact, since I have been exact throughout —
on the fourteenth day I decided nothing, because a thing decided in the hour you first understand its
size is not a decision, it is a flinch, and I have spent my life refusing to let my hand flinch.

But I went up by way of the Office, at a shift when I knew it would be empty, and I stood in the map room
where the master charts hang, and I looked at the place where Deck Nought is drawn as solid hull — a
clean grey block of it, laid down by some cartographer a hundred years dead, in the good steady line I
have been able to draw since I was fourteen — and I understood, finally and all at once, what my trade
had actually been the whole time, under the surveying and the tea and the confirmed doorways.

Somebody has drawn that grey block by hand, every generation, on purpose, knowing.

And it was going to be me next. That was what Vosse had been unable to say and unwilling to spare me:
not *the ship is coasting*, which is only arithmetic, but *the editing is a person, and the person is
about to be you, and I cannot tell you whether to lay the block down or lift it, because the founders
took even that from me and gave it to whoever is holding the key.*

I was holding the key. It was against my ribs, warm now from being carried, where a wound would be.
