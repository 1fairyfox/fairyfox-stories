On the nineteenth day I went to Vosse and asked him to come down with me.

He was in the small office with the tea going cold, doing what he had done every morning of my life:
initialling confirmations. Doorways. Bulkheads. The unglamorous ninety-nine per cent of a trade that
consists of writing *yes, it is still there* on a form. He looked at me over his glasses for a while.

"Down," he said.

"To Deck Nought."

He put the pen in the tray, squared it, and said, "I have been once."

"I know."

"Forty-one years ago." He said it evenly, and then he said the rest of it, which I had not known and
which I think he had not said aloud to any living person: "I was forty-five. I was down there eleven
minutes. I have not been able to make myself go back, and I have carried the key every day since, and I
want you to understand that those two facts are the same fact, and that I have known it about myself the
whole time."

I want to be exact about what I felt, because I had come to his office with an argument prepared and I
did not use one word of it. What I felt was that I had been standing next to a man for seventeen years
and had been reading, the entire time, a version of him that I had drawn myself, and that mine was
eight metres short.

"Come down," I said. "I'll walk you."

---

It takes an old man a long time to get down the 14-aft corridor and through a hatch. I am not going to
make that noble. It took forty minutes, and he was grey at the end of it, and he would not take my arm
until the last two metres and then took it without comment, and I have decided to write that down rather
than leave it out, because leaving it out is exactly the trade I am trying to stop practising.

He stood in the cold and looked down the length of it. One hundred and forty point oh four metres of
solid hull. Then he went, slowly, to the observation blister, and stopped, because I had already
brought the chair round to face the window, and he saw it before he saw the window.

The chair on Deck Nought is worn smooth.

I had noticed it the first day and filed it as an observation about materials. On the nineteenth day I
made myself do the arithmetic instead, which is the only prayer I have and I had been declining to say
it. That chair is four hundred and eleven years old. Nobody lives on Deck Nought. Nobody maintains it.
Nobody has ever sat in it for pleasure. The arms of it are worn down to a shine that takes hands —
hundreds of hours of hands, gripping — and it faces the true instruments, and it is the only chair in
the room.

Eleven people, before me, sat in that chair long enough to polish it. Each of them, I am now certain,
believed themselves to be the only one who ever had.

Vosse sat down in it. I heard him let his breath out.

"Nobody has ever asked me what it was like," he said, to the window.

"I'm asking."

And he told me. I am going to keep most of it, the way I kept my share of the First Cartographer, but I
will set down the one line, because it is the reason for everything I did afterward and it would be
dishonest to make the reason sound like mine.

He said: "It was like finding out the whole ship is a room with one person in it. And then finding out
the person is you. And then having to go up and make tea."

---

The folder is unlabelled. It lives in Chart Room 2 in the third drawer with the superseded surveys, and
it is not locked, and it is not hidden, and it never has been, because the founders did not build this
out of locks. They built it out of the correct assumption that almost nobody measures a corridor twice.

Eleven filings. Four hundred years. Every one of them the same corridor, Deck 14-aft, frame 90 to 118,
and every one of them the same number: one hundred and sixty-nine metres and change, filed, initialled,
superseded, and put in a drawer under a survey about a doorway.

None of them signed. All of them, of course, perfectly identifiable to anybody trained in this Office,
because we are taught to write our capitals off the First Cartographer's hand and we each fail at it in
our own particular way, and a cartographer's hand is more of a name than a name is. The sixth is my
grandmother's. I have known her hand since I was six years old; she taught it to me at a table with my
elbows in the wrong place. She filed it in 371, when she was thirty-four, and she lived another forty-one
years after that, and she came to my apprenticing, and she held my face in her two hands and told me I
had a good steady line, and she never said one word.

She sat in that chair. She polished a piece of it. She went up and made tea.

I have thought about being angry with her. I find that I am not, and I have examined that honestly for
signs of cowardice, and I do not think it is cowardice. I think I am not angry with her because I now
know precisely how the room is built, and it is built so that each person in it is alone, and a person
alone does not make a choice. A person alone flinches, and then spends forty years calling the flinch a
decision, and initials confirmations every morning with a very steady hand.

Eleven flinches, laid end to end across four centuries, look exactly like a policy.

That was the thing I could not accept, in the end. Not the coasting — the coasting is arithmetic, and I
had made my peace with arithmetic before I could read. Not the painted horizon, either; the First
Cartographer had made her case for that at the end of a sixty-year education and I have not been able to
find the flaw in it and I have looked. What I could not accept was that the most consequential act
performed on this ship in four hundred years had been performed eleven times, in solitude, in the cold,
by frightened people who each thought they were the first and none of whom ever got to talk to anyone
about it.

That is not mercy. Mercy is a choice. That was eleven people being handed a weight in the dark and told
it was theirs and nobody's else, forever, and every one of them doing the only thing a human being does
when they are alone with something that size, which is the smallest possible thing.

---

So. What I recorded, and what I left as hull. I will set it down plainly, and you may weigh it; I am
not going to argue for it, because I have noticed that a person who argues for a decision has not
finished making it.

I filed the survey. Deck 14-aft, frame 90 to 118: one hundred and sixty-nine point one one metres. True,
exact, to the millimetre, with my working attached, and I put it in the unlabelled folder in the third
drawer under a superseded survey about a doorway, and I am the twelfth.

I signed it. Sela Renn, 411, in my own hand, badly, the way I have always failed at the capitals.

Then I went into the map room at a shift when it was empty, and I took a rule and a pen, and where Deck
Nought lies I drew the grey block. Solid hull. By hand. In the good steady line I have had since I was
fourteen, that my grandmother told me was good and steady and did not tell me what for.

I want to be exact about this, since I have been exact throughout: the block I drew is identical to the
block that was there. Same dimensions, same fill, same hand — the Office has trained twelve generations
to draw the same block, which is either the most beautiful thing about my trade or the most frightening,
and I no longer think you are required to pick. It is the same block. It is a completely different
object. The one that was there, I inherited. This one, I chose, at thirty-one, in an empty room, with the
key in my pocket and the broadcast panel forty metres away and nothing in the world stopping me but my
own arithmetic.

I did not broadcast. Here is my reason, and it is mine, not hers: I am one person, and this is eleven
thousand, and a thing done by one person in an afternoon to eleven thousand is not the truth arriving.
It is a flinch with a very large radius. If this ship is ever going to be told, it will be told by more
than one frightened cartographer with a good line and a bad week, and there is currently no mechanism
on the Ark Meridian by which more than one person can be in this room at a time.

So I built one. That is the whole of what I did, and it is small, and I have decided it is not nothing.

---

I made a chart.

Not of the hull. The hull is charted; the hull is the easy part; the hull has been charted eleven times
by better hands than mine and it changed nothing at all, and that is the fact I finally learned to read.

I charted the room.

I drew Deck Nought true — one hundred and forty point oh four metres, the blister, the housing that was
made to be found, the plain shelf, the reader, the plate, the window, and the chair, and I marked the
chair, and I wrote beside it what the arms of it are worth in hours. And then, on the same sheet,
because a map is a record of what is actually there and there was never a hull on this deck, only
people, I drew the other thing.

Twelve names.

It took me nine days to get them. The hands are in the folder; the hands are in the Office rolls; a
cartographer's failure at the capitals is a fingerprint, and I have spent my life being the sort of
person who checks. Two of them I am not certain of and I have marked them uncertain, with my reasoning,
because a map that hides its error bars is a poster. The sixth is Aurelie Renn, 371, and I wrote *my
grandmother* beside it, which is not cartographic practice, and I am not sorry.

The eleventh is Ilkka Vosse, 370 to 411, forty-one years, eleven minutes.

The twelfth is me.

I put it on the shelf on Deck Nought beside the reader, under the plate that says *For whoever measures
true*, and I did not seal it, or hide it, or add a word of instruction, because the founders were right
about that part and I have the humility to copy a thing that works. The First Cartographer left the next
person an argument. That was more than anyone had ever been left, and it was still not enough, because
an argument is a voice and a voice is one more way of being alone in a cold room.

I left them a map. The whole point of a map is that somebody was here before you and wrote down what
they found, so that you do not have to be the first, ever again, at anything.

The thirteenth person to walk down that corridor is going to sit in that chair. I cannot stop that and I
would not; the chair is the price. But they are going to sit down in it and look up and see that the
arms are worn smooth by twelve pairs of hands, and know it, on arrival, in the first minute, with the
names.

That is the honest map. That is the one I drew. It is for one person, and I will never meet them, and
they are the only reader I have ever had in mind while drawing anything.

---

Vosse retired at the end of the month. He filed the form himself, and initialled it, and squared the
pen in the tray, and gave me the Office and did not make a speech. At the door he stopped and said,
"Which did you file? The block, or the number?"

"Both," I said.

He stood there a moment. Then he nodded once, the way he nods at a confirmed doorway, and said, "Good,"
and went home, and I understand that this was the largest thing he has ever said to me.

---

There is one more measurement and then I am finished.

I could have closed the corridor. I want that understood, because it is the piece that will be held
against me and it should be. Deck 14-aft, frame 90 to 118: chart says one hundred and sixty-one, true is
one hundred and sixty-nine point one one. Eight metres of visible, checkable, arithmetical lie sitting in
a public corridor on a residential deck where any apprentice with a tape and a grudge could trip over it,
and I am now Master Cartographer and it would take me one afternoon and a renumbering of the frames to
make that corridor reconcile forever.

Eleven people, in four hundred years, every one of them competent, every one of them terrified, every one
of them holding the key — and not one of them closed it.

I know why now. It took me until the nineteenth day and an old man in a chair. The grey block on the
master chart is the kind story; the eight metres in the corridor is the door. They are drawn by the same
hand, in the same office, on the same morning, on purpose, and they are not a contradiction. They are the
whole design. *Here is a horizon to walk toward, and here, in the wall, is a discrepancy, left exactly
where a person who cannot stop counting will find it.* Every generation, the Office paints the sky and
leaves the crack. That is the trade. That is what my grandmother's steady line was actually for.

So I left it. Eight metres, uncorrected, in a public corridor, for as long as I hold this office.

The routine resurvey of Deck 14-aft comes up on a twenty-two year rotation. I have not touched the
schedule. It will fall in 433.

Emil Tolver, compartment 41, nine-spinward, will be thirty-one.

He has copied my drift diagram out by hand in pencil, badly, with the arrow going the right way, and
hung it over a doorframe with a number cut into it that I gave his great-grandmother out loud, well,
without hesitating, and I knew it was a story when I said it and I would say it again tomorrow. He is a
boy who checks. I have watched him check. If he comes into this Office, and stays, and one day walks that
corridor with a tape because the number in his head will not sit still — then in 433 a young man is going
to find eight metres that should not be there, and open a hatch, and go down into the cold, and sit in a
chair.

And there will be a map on the shelf. And it will have twelve names on it, and one of them will be mine,
in my own bad capitals, and he will know before he has finished sitting down that he is not the first,
and that this is a thing people have carried, and that he is allowed to come and find the one person
alive who is holding the other end of it.

That is all I could draw. It is not the truth and it is not a lie; those were never the two things, and
it cost me my whole faith in my trade to learn it. A map is a thing one person makes and hands to
another, and the only real question — the one the First Cartographer could not draw and neither can I —
is who you are making it for.

I am making it for him. I have never met him. He is nine.

I have drawn worse maps for better reasons.
