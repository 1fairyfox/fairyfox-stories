Here is how you ask at the Cage.

You do not walk up the front steps between the iron birds and knock, because the front steps are for
buyers, and buyers arrive in carriages with a Magister's invitation folded in their glove. You go
round to Coldharbour Lane, to the consignment window in the Cage's north wall — a slot of brass and
glass no wider than a coffin lid, where anyone in Cinderwick with a curio and a nerve may offer it up
to the Ordinal for sale. There's a bell. You ring it. Then you stand in the lane with your collar up
and your name filed off, and you wait to see what the wall thinks of you.

I stood there for four minutes. I know because I counted, the way I count everything I can't control.

I had spent the day being careful in the ways that don't show. I'd sold the snuffbox in the
Saltmarket for eleven guineas, which was three under fair and worth it for the speed. I'd walked
three streets I had no reason to walk to see who walked them with me, and nobody did, which meant
either nobody was following me or somebody very good was. I'd taken the card to a paper-man off Quill
Street, who wet his thumb, held it to the lamp, sniffed it, and told me it was Vestry laid, milled in
the Reach, sold to eleven houses in Cinderwick, of which nine were the Ordinal's. He charged me two
shillings for that and I'd have paid ten. Nine of eleven. There's a comfort in a number. It tells you
you're not mad; it just doesn't tell you anything you can spend.

The window opened. There was a clerk behind it the way there is a spider behind a web — present,
uninterested, entirely at home.

"Consignment?" he said.

"Enquiry," I said.

"We don't take enquiries."

"You'll take this one." I put the card face up on the brass. I didn't let go of it, either, which was
a small pointless thing that made me feel better and told him more than I liked.

He looked at the card. He did not look at my face, which is the part I remember, because everyone
looks at your face; a face is the only free thing in this city. He looked at the card for perhaps two
seconds, and then he reached under his counter and slid back a small square of pasteboard with a
number printed on it — 214 — and said, "The Green Ledger. Nine o'clock. Take the number in with you,"
and shut the window, and the lane was very quiet, and I stood there holding a bidder's chit for an
auction I had not agreed to attend.

That's the thing about the Cage. It doesn't argue. It just makes room for you, and you find you've
walked in.

---

The Green Ledger is an oyster-and-gin room halfway up the hill, all brass rails and green glass, where
the middling sort of criminal goes to be seen not being criminal. I got there at half eight and took
the corner table with my back to the wall and both exits in front of me — the door to the street and
the door to the yard, and a third one nobody counts, which is the dumbwaiter, and a person my size can
count it. Then I ate six oysters I didn't want, because a woman eating alone is furniture and a woman
sitting alone is a problem, and I have never in eleven years been a problem in a public room.

He arrived at nine exactly. Not a second before, which is the mark of a man who's been standing round
the corner waiting for a clock.

"Miss Vale," he said. "Pell. If you'll allow it."

Pell was a damp, apologetic sort of man in a good coat that didn't fit the rest of him — the coat of a
man dressed by someone else's money. He had a clerk's stoop, a wet mouth, and the softest hands I've
ever seen on a person who was not a baby, and he sat down across from me without asking, which is the
only bold thing he did all night, and I could smell the fear coming off him like damp off a wall.

That was interesting. I'd come expecting a spider. They'd sent me a moth.

"You'll take a drink," he said.

"I'll take the name of whoever wrote my card."

"Ah." Pell folded the soft hands. "My principal anticipated. My principal says — and I do apologise,
Miss Vale, I only carry the words, I don't own them — my principal says that a name is a thing you may
purchase from him at the end, and not before, and that you of all people know what a name is worth."

He said it the way a boy recites a lesson, eyes up and to the left, and I understood then that Pell
did not know what my card had said, and did not want to, and had spent the walk over here hoping I'd
be somebody else.

I let the silence sit. Silence is cheaper than questions and it buys more.

"There's a lot," Pell said, at last, into it. "Coming to the night auction at the Cage, nine nights
from now. Lot forty-one. The Cinderwick Nightingale."

I know curios. I have made my living on curios for eleven years and I have never once been able to say
that name out loud without wanting to hold the thing. It's a bird. A little mechanical bird, brass and
enamel, no bigger than a fist, and what it does — the one modest impossible thing it does — is sing
back. Any sound it has ever heard, it can give you again, whole and clean, in the voice that made it.
Wind the key and it will sing back a violin, a bell, a scream, a woman saying *evening* on her way out
a window. Everything it has ever heard, it still has.

And here is the part that made my mouth go dry, and Pell watched it happen, and did not enjoy it,
which I'll say for him.

The Nightingale has sat in the Cage's own vault for forty years. Unsold. Not for sale. A house curio,
brought up for the viewing and put back, the way a family shows a portrait it will never part with.
Forty years in the vault under the Ordinal's auction house, in the room where the Ordinal does its
business, listening.

"They're selling it," I said.

"They're *showing* it," Pell said. "Lot forty-one is listed and will be viewed and will not, my
principal believes, be permitted to actually leave. That is not your difficulty. Your difficulty is
the taking of it, out of the Cage, on the night of the auction, and the placing of it in my hands by
dawn." He wet his lips. "My principal will pay four hundred guineas."

Four hundred guineas is not a fee. Four hundred guineas is a small house on a good street and a
different life, and both of us knew it, and he watched me hear it.

"No," I said.

Pell blinked. "Miss Vale—"

"You've come to the best finder in Cinderwick and asked her to rob the one building in this city that
cannot be robbed, on the one night of the year it is fullest of Ordinal men, for a bird that isn't
even for sale, and you've opened at four hundred guineas." I ate my last oyster. "That's not a job.
That's a stage, and I'm being invited to stand on it. You can tell your principal that Juno Vale is
flattered and busy."

And I meant it. I want that written down somewhere, because of what happens next, and because I would
like the record to show that for one whole minute of my life I was the sensible woman my mother
wanted.

Pell had gone the colour of the tablecloth. He looked at his soft hands. Then he took a folded paper
from his coat, and unfolded it, and turned it round on the wood so it faced me, and put one finger on
it to hold it flat, and did not look at it while I read.

It was a ledger page. Ordinal stock — the ruled kind, the watermarked kind, with the little crest in
the corner that they think is a comfort. A page of receipts from eleven years ago, entered in the
Cage's own hand: monies paid out, informations received. And there, in the third column, in ink gone
brown as old blood, in a clerk's tidy copperplate, sat a night-mail ticket, and a sum, and a name.

*Wren, Junia. Delivered.*

Not *taken*. Not *arrested*. **Delivered.** Somebody had been paid for me, and somebody had written it
down like a delivery of coal, and the paper had been sitting in a drawer in that building for eleven
years while I walked past it in the dark and thought I was safe because nobody said the name out loud.

The room went a long way off. I could hear the brass rail, the oysters, a man laughing at the bar. I
watched my own hand on the table not shake, which took everything I had, and I have never in my life
worked so hard at anything as I worked at that hand.

"There is one page," Pell said, very quietly, to the tablecloth. "My principal is not a cruel man. He
is a *thorough* one. The page comes to you with the four hundred, on delivery of the bird. And I am to
say — I'm sorry, Miss Vale, I only carry the words — I am to say that there are other pages, and other
drawers, and that they keep."

So there it was. Not a hire. A leash, held out politely, with a bow on it.

And I want to tell you that I got up and walked out and left the Reach for good, because that was the
move, that was so obviously the move that a child could have made it, and instead I heard myself say,
in a voice I recognised and did not much care for:

"Nine nights."

"Nine nights."

"And the viewing?"

"Three nights before. Lot forty-one will be under glass in the long gallery, and the gallery is open
to the trade." Pell folded the ledger page away, and I watched it go the way you watch a surgeon put
his knife back in the roll. "My principal thought you might wish to look at it first. He said —" and
here Pell frowned, because this was the part of his lesson that he hadn't understood, and it was
plainly bothering him — "he said to tell you that the bird has been in that vault a long time. He
said you'd know what that means, and that you would want it whether or not he paid you a farthing."

I sat in the Green Ledger with the taste of salt in my mouth and I did know what it meant, and the
knowing was worse than the ledger page, and it went through me like a picklock finding the last pin.

Forty years in the vault. Under the auction house. In the room where they do the business.

*Delivered*, said the page, in a tidy hand, and somebody had stood in that room eleven years ago and
said my name out loud to collect on it — said *Junia Wren* to a clerk of the Ordinal in a vaulted room
with a little brass bird on a shelf, listening.

It heard him. Whoever he was. Whatever he sounded like. That bird has his voice in it, still, whole
and clean, waiting for somebody to wind the key.

"Tell your principal," I said, and my hand was steady and my heart was going like a watch-whistle,
"that I'll take the job."

Pell nodded, and got up, and put on his hat, and was three steps toward the door when he stopped and
came back, and leaned down, and said the only thing he said that night that came from Pell and not
from the pocket of that ill-fitting coat.

"I'd have run," he said. "For what it's worth. I'd have run."

"I know," I said. "That's why they sent you and not somebody I'd have listened to."

He left. I stayed, and paid for the oysters, and sat with my back to the wall counting exits I had no
intention of using, because I already knew what I was going to do, and I want to be honest about it
even now, especially now.

The sensible read is that I took the job because they had my name in a drawer. It's a good read. It'll
hold water in any court in the Reach and I'd swear to it under oath.

But I'd have taken it for the bird.

Nine nights. One vault, one auction, one impossible door, and a small brass creature that has been
sitting in the dark for forty years holding the sound of the man who sold me.

*A door only you can open,* the card said.

They were right. That was the trouble with all of it, from the first line to the last — every single
thing they told me about myself was true.
