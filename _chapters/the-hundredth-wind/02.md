A wind you are chasing and a wind you are carrying are two different weights on a camel, and the
second is the heavier. I carried none east — the ninety-nine were long gone into the quiet, and the
hundredth was not yet mine to carry — so I rode light for once, and I will tell you it did not suit
me. A catcher with an empty glass-rack is a man with nothing to lose corked to his saddle, and a man
with nothing to lose is only interesting to himself.

The road east out of Zerat runs three days through the salt-pans before it remembers how to be a
road, and for the first of them the child said nothing at all. She rode the way she had stood in the
doorway of the caravanserai — braced, both hands to the pommel, lifted a little off the saddle as if
the camel's long sway were a swell that might pitch her over a rail into the sea. There is no sea on
the salt-pans. There is only the white, and the heat standing off it in sheets, and a horizon that
lies to you the way all horizons lie. But she watched the empty air the whole first day as though it
were a crowded room, and once, when an idle afternoon gust came up off the pan and turned the corner
of her grey collar, she made a sound I had not heard from any grown soul in forty years of the trade:
a small caught frightened sound, the sound you would make if the flat ground under you took a breath.

Then she put two fingers to the place at her throat where the wind had touched her, and left them
there, and rode the rest of the day like a woman keeping something in.

I said nothing about it. A catcher learns when to let the weather do his talking.

By the second morning I could not keep the trade behind my teeth any longer — I never could, it is my
whole affliction — and so I did what I have done in guild-halls and gutters both, and taught. There
was no crowd to teach at, only the one small grey person swaying along at my stirrup, and I have
since thought that this was the first honest lesson I gave in thirty years, precisely because nobody
had paid to hear it.

"Three laws," I told her, and held up the fingers, because the hand believes a thing the ear will
argue with. "Learn these three and you know more than most who take the name. The first you have
already seen me keep. Every wind that blows has one true name, and the catch is nothing but the
speaking of it into the mouth of the glass. Wrong name, and the finest bottle on the Road holds a
wind no better than a sieve holds soup. Right name" — I snapped the cork-hand shut — "and it cannot
help but come, the way you cannot help but turn when your own is called across a market. Names are
the whole of the craft. Everything else is stools and patter."

She considered this with a gravity that eleven years had no business owning. "And the wind we are
going for," she said. "Its name."

"Is the one law's one exception, and we will come to it when I am braver. Second law." I would not be
hurried off my own performance. "A bottled wind is proved once, and once only, at the buyer's own
threshold. You carry it corked from the catch to his door, and there — on his stone, under his own
eye — you draw the cork the single time, so that he sees the wind stir in the glass and knows for
certain it is live before ever a coin changes hands. Then you seal it, and it is his. A wind unproved
is a jar of ordinary air, and any liar alive can sell that. We are not liars." I let that sit in the
white heat a moment, pleased with it, not yet knowing what it would cost me to have said it. "We
prove at the threshold. Bottled and delivered, the contracts say, and the proving is the delivering.
No threshold, no sale."

"And the third."

"The third is the one the young ones laugh at and the old ones weep over." I dropped my voice the way
you drop a voice to make a child lean in, and she leaned, up on her toes even in the saddle. "Never
sell a wind you would miss. There. You may laugh."

She did not laugh. "That is not a law of the craft," she said. "That is a law about you."

Out of the mouths of the Emir's instruments. I have been corrected by chandlers' guilds and
customs-men and my own better before I lost her, and none of them ever landed it as flat as that
grey child on a camel, who had never in her life owned a wind to miss, and knew the shape of missing
one anyway.

Because — and here is the thing I had not been told, and had been too vain to ask — she knew the
ninety-nine. Not from any label. She had grown up among them, in the vault at the heart of the House
of Still Air, where the bottles stand in their thousands of held breaths and knock, very softly,
against the glass in the dark; and she had lain awake under that knocking her whole childhood the way
another child lies awake under rain, and had learned every one of my life's catches by the sound it
made wanting out. She told me, that second night, with the fire between us and no wind for a hundred
miles to trouble it, which of my winds sang and which of them only wept. The dawn wind off the
Kessa dunes, she said, turned over three times an hour and sighed like a sleeper. The grief wind out
of Talibar never moved at all, only pressed, steady, the way a hand presses a door it has given up
on. She had names for them that were not their names — the Sigher, the Presser, the Little Heresy that
snuffed the lamps, which was mine of that very morning and already knocking in the west with the rest.

"They want out," she said, and it was not a complaint. She said it the way you would report the tide.
"All of them. All the time. You put them in glass so people could hear them, and now they only ever
knock." She turned her two fingers over in the firelight, the ones the wind had touched. "I have
never once been outside in weather," she said, "until this road. I did not know it would feel like a
hand."

I looked at the fire, and my own hand had gone, on its own account, to the breast pocket sewn under
my shirt, to the cracked horn stopper that lives there, older than my fame, and I took it away again
before she could mark it. A catcher's hand is a liar of long standing. It goes to the true thing
while the mouth is busy elsewhere.

We came to the town on the fourth day, and knew from a mile off that the wind had been there before
us, because the doors were open.

Every door. That is how you read the Unbottled's passage on the Road — not by wreckage, for it breaks
nothing, but by *openness*, a whole town standing ajar in the noon like a house after a burial when
the mourners have gone and nobody yet has the heart to shut the place up. Shutters wide. Coffers,
chests, the poor-box on the shrine-step, the letter-satchel of the runner who had been passing
through — all of it opened, aired, its keeping undone, and the townsfolk out in the square in the
white light doing the thing that town was doing, which was standing very still among one another's
secrets and deciding what to do now that they were had.

For the wind opens shut things, and a town has two kinds. I saw both in the one square, and so will
you, because I am a catcher and I read weather the way a reeve reads a ledger, and this was the wind's
whole doubleness laid out for teaching.

Here was the first kind: a woman with a letter in her hand that was not hers to have, and a man three
paces off who could not meet her eye, and between them a marriage of nine years coming quietly apart
in public, its little door blown open, nothing in it now that would ever shut again. Mischief, the
towns would call that, and curse the wind for a mischief-maker, and they would not be wrong. It had
opened a thing that a kinder weather would have left decently closed, and two people would grieve for
it who had done the wind no harm.

And here, not ten paces on, was the second kind. A cellar door stood open under the grain-factor's
house — the fat man of that town, the one who weighs — and on the cobbles where the wind had laid it
out for the reading was the short-weight beam of his salt-scale, the filed one, the crooked one he
had kept in the dark for years while the town paid full coin for light measure; and beside it, wept
over now by a grey-headed woman, the proof at last of a girl long dead and long blamed, who had gone
to her grave a thief in that town's mouth for a theft that had been his. The wind had opened that too.
The same weather. The same laughing gust that broke the marriage had, in the one breath, aired a
buried wrong that no court in nine years had troubled to dig for, and given a dead girl back her name
in the square where they had taken it.

Mischief and judgment in the one wind, and no telling which it means to be, because it means neither.
It only opens. What comes out is the town's, not the wind's. I have thought since that this is the
truest thing I know about weather, and about a good deal else besides.

Noor stood in the middle of that opened town with her chin up and her fingers at her throat, reading
the square the way she read the vault in the dark, and she said, not to me, "It doesn't choose."

"No," I said. "It doesn't."

"Then how do you catch a thing that doesn't want, and doesn't choose, and has a different name in
every mouth?"

I did not answer her, because the honest answer was that I did not yet know, and the vain answer was
beneath even me in that square. My hand had gone again to the pocket and the cracked horn, in a town
where every shut thing but the one I carried had been opened to the noon; and somewhere west of us,
running its bright indifferent circuit toward the towns of my own young years, the hundredth wind
laughed its way through another set of doors, and did not yet know my scent.

It would. But that is the next day's telling, and the next day's cost.
