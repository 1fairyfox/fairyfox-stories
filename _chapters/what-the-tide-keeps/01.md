The sea gives things back. It is the first thing you learn on this coast and the last thing you
believe, and my whole working life has been the room where the two meet.

I keep the ledger above the chandlery. My father kept it before me for a while, and before him a
woman named Sorrel Aim whose handwriting still runs the first two hundred pages, and before her a
line of keepers going back further than the paper does. The room is small and smells of tar and
cold iron and the particular damp of things that have been in the water. There is a desk, a lamp,
a long shelf of the bound ledgers, and a set of brass scales I use more out of ceremony than need.
People bring me what the tide returns. I weigh it, measure it, describe it, and write it down. That
is all. I have been doing it for nineteen years and I am good at it, which is a strange thing to be
good at, and I have never once let myself wonder aloud what any of it means.

That morning the fog had come in overnight and stayed, the way it does in the last weeks of the
year, so that the harbour was a rumour and the foghorn out on the point counted the minutes for us
all. I had three returns before noon. I remember them because I remember all of them; it is the
part of the work I cannot help.

The first was an oar, come back to the Pauls two days after they lost it — ordinary, that, the sea
returns small things quickly and without drama, the way you'd hand back a dropped glove. I weighed
it, noted the crack along the loom that would have been why it went over, and wrote it in. Ordinary.
I like ordinary. Ordinary is the ledger doing what a ledger is for.

The second was a christening cup, silver, black with the sea, that no one in Cape Herring could put
a name to. That happens. A thing comes back and there is no one left to claim it, the loss so old
the loser is gone, and then it is mine to hold until the record finds a hand for it or doesn't. I
cleaned the tarnish from the rim enough to read the initials — *T.A.H.* — and left the rest black,
because it is not my place to make a returned thing look like it was never taken. I set it on the
shelf with the others. There is a whole shelf of the unclaimed. I do not think about the shelf.

The third was Halvard Munn, who is not a return but might as well be, since he comes up my stairs
most weeks to sit and not say the name of the wife the sea took in the spring and has not, so far,
given back — not her, not so much as a shoe. He brought me a flask of the bad coffee from the harbour
café as though it were owed, and drank most of it himself, and looked at the shelf of the unclaimed
for a while, and left. I let him. That is also part of the work, the part that isn't written down.

I tell you all this so you understand the shape of an ordinary day, and how completely I trusted it.

I was closing the ledger — I close it at noon, I have always closed it at noon, my father closed it
at noon — when the stair creaked under a weight that was trying not to make it creak, and Osk
Berentsen came into the room with his cap in his two hands.

Osk is a careful man. He fishes the far side of the point where the good crews won't bother and the
bad crews can't, and he has never in his life brought me a return, because a man like Osk keeps what
the sea gives him and says nothing, on the sound theory that the sea keeps count either way. So I
knew, before he had crossed the room, that whatever this was, it had frightened him enough to bring
it up my stairs in daylight.

He put a bundle on the desk. Oilskin, folded the way you fold a thing you don't want to look at
again, tied with a length of tarred twine in a knot I recognised — a fisherman's knot, but tied
left-handed, the way only one person I ever knew tied them.

"Where," I said.

"Landward side of the breakwater." He turned his cap. "This morning. In the wrack, above the line."
Above the tide line. Which meant the sea had set it there gently, on purpose, the way it does with
the things it has thought about.

"Whose is it?"

And Osk Berentsen, who is a careful man, looked at me the way you look at a person you are about to
do a small unkindness to for their own good, and said, "I think you'd best open it yourself, Maren.
I'll not say. I could be wrong." He was not a man who was ever wrong about the sea. He put his cap
back on, which was how I knew he was leaving, and at the door he stopped and did not turn around and
said, "It's got no rust on it. None. Nineteen years it'd have to have been down there, by my
reckoning, and there's not a spot of rust on it." Then the stair took his weight, honestly this
time, all the way down, and the door to the street let in one breath of fog and closed.

I did not open it. Not then. I want to be truthful about that, because everything that came after
turns on it: I sat with the bundle on my desk and my hands flat on either side of it and I did not
untie the knot. I told myself I was closing for noon. I closed for noon. I wrote the day's three
returns into the ledger in my small exact hand — oar, cup, and then a blank line where the fourth
should go, because I do not record a return I have not weighed and measured and described, and I had
not weighed this one, and I would not, not yet.

I left the blank line and I hated it. Nineteen years and there is not one unfinished line in any of
my ledgers. I balance them the way my father taught me, so that anyone could open any page after I
am gone and know exactly what the sea took from this town and exactly what it gave back, down to the
gram. A blank line is a debt. A blank line is the one thing the room is for keeping out.

Below me the foghorn counted. On the desk the oilskin held its shape, and the knot Emil used to tie
left-handed because he thought it looked cleverer than the way I taught him held its shape too, and
I sat in the smell of tar and cold iron and the water, and for the first time in nineteen years I
was afraid of the ledger — not of what it might not hold, which is the fear I had built my whole
life around, but of what it might.
