Basri Yel has been the name in wind-catching for forty years: ninety-nine winds taken, named, and
bottled, each with a story he tells better every season. The craft is real — the stand, the glass, the
patience, the naming — and so is the fame. What is not quite real is the first story, the one the
other ninety-eight are stacked on: the Dawn Wind, the First Catch, the morning that made his name.

## What he wants

- **Wants:** the hundredth catch — the legend sealed, the name finished, cut somewhere weather can't
  wear it off.
- **The trouble:** the only wind left worth a legend is the one that answers to nothing but the truth,
  and the truth is the one thing Basri has spent forty years not saying.

## His place in the story

The narrator and the lock the whole tale turns in. Vain, funny, genuinely masterful, and hollow
exactly where the legend is — a man who performs even to himself, hunting the one quarry that cannot
be performed at. His hand goes, more often than he'd admit, to a cracked bottle-stopper in his breast
pocket that he has carried since a sandstorm forty years back.

## Voice

Showman's patter, built like a market-stall pitch — flourish, misdirection, the pause before the
reveal — thinning, chapter by chapter, toward plain speech. (His own narration.)
