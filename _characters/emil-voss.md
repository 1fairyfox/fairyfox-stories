Emil Voss drowned at nineteen, off the Cape Herring breakwater, on a clear evening that had no
business taking anyone. He was the warm one — quick to laugh, quicker to forgive, careless in the
way people can afford to be when they assume there will be more time. He and Maren argued the
afternoon he died; she has kept the shape of that argument for twenty years the way you keep a
splinter you never dig out.

The sea has never returned his body. For nineteen years it returned nothing of his at all — which
Maren took, privately, as a verdict. What it finally gives back, and when, is the hinge of
*[What the Tide Keeps](../stories/what-the-tide-keeps/)*.

He appears in memory, in the ledger's silences, and in what the tide keeps — present in the book far
more than his few living scenes suggest.
