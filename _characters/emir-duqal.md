The Emir Duqal lives in a white house built so that no air moves in it, at the heart of which is a
vault where ninety-nine bottled winds knock against glass. He did not buy them to hear them. A storm
took his son's ship, years ago, and the Emir drew from that morning a merciful arithmetic: cage every
wind, one by one, and no storm can ever again take anyone's son. He believes this the way other men
believe in charity.

## What he wants

- **Wants:** the weather, ended — every wind in glass, starting with the one no catcher has ever
  taken. He pays honestly, in full, and keeps the letter of every bargain.
- **The trouble:** his patience has a floor. When the catch falters he comes with iron, tar, and nets
  that need no name — and a wind taken that way doesn't live in the bottle. It stills. Dead air.

## His place in the story

The antagonist who believes he is mercy itself — no cackle, no cruelty for its own sake, just grief
with a budget behind it. The vise of the tale closes with his nets, and the ending does not defeat him
so much as *answer* him: the letter of his own commission is kept to the last word, and what the freed
weather finally carries out to him is not a punishment.

## Voice

Courteous, exact, contract-precise; the quietest person in any room and the hardest to refuse. (Seen
through Basri's narration.)
