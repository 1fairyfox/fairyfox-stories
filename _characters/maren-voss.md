Maren Voss keeps the ledger above the chandlery, and has for nineteen years. She is in her late
forties, spare and exact, with hands that have gutted more fish than she could count and a memory
that forgives nothing — least of all herself.

## What she wants (and needs)

- **Wants:** for the sea to return her brother Emil, who drowned off the breakwater when they were
  young and their last words were an argument she started. She keeps the ledger, in part, as a
  demand made to the water: *account for him.*
- **Needs:** to stop keeping the ledger against the world — to let a thing go unrecorded, unclaimed,
  unforgiven, and survive it.

## The flaw that costs her

Control. Maren accounts for everything because she cannot bear what can't be accounted for. It made
her rigid with Emil; it has made her solitary since. When the sea finally returns something of his,
her instinct is to *file* it — and filing it is exactly how she has kept from grieving for twenty years.

## Contradiction

The most unforgiving person in Cape Herring is also the one everyone trusts to hold what they can't
bear to keep. People bring her their returned things because she will not flinch. She reads this as
duty. It is also hiding.

## Voice

Dry, precise, watchful. She notices weights and edges and the exact colour of things. She undercounts
her own feelings and overcounts everyone else's. First person, past tense.
