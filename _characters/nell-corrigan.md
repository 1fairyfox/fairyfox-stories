Nell Corrigan opens doors for a living, at the hours when doors matter most — the two o'clock
call, the locked-out and the locked-in, the snapped key in a stranger's cold hand. She is fast,
quiet, and asks nothing; a good lock, she'll tell you, is just a problem that hasn't been listened
to yet. She's somewhere in her thirties, works out of a van that smells of oil and old coffee, and
carries herself like a woman who has already decided how any given night will disappoint her.

## What she wants (and needs)

- **Wants:** to be left alone to work — to owe no one, be owed nothing, and keep the ledger of her
  life closed and even.
- **Needs:** to stop bolting the door on the people who could still love her — starting with the
  one she shut herself, and starting by letting a door be opened *for* her, which is the only lock
  she's never once been able to pick.

## The flaw that costs her

She mistakes a bolt for safety. Nell will open any door in the city for a stranger and turn the
lock on anyone who gets close, then call the silence peace. She's spent three years telling the
story where her brother *left* — because the true story, the one with her hand on the bolt, is the
one door she can't stand at.

## Voice

Wry, quick, low to the ground; notices exits and prices and tells; the fear kept one wisecrack
back from the surface. First person, past tense.
