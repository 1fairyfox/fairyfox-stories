# Book-notes — The Cartographer of Decks (internal)

- **Author-persona:** Idris Okonkwo-Vance — cool ironist / documentarian. **Style:** plain,
  cool-literary; clean lines, emotion in the margins, strangeness landing because the reporting is
  trustworthy. Hold it: precise, understated. Sela narrates as a *report*, and keeps saying so
  ("I want to be exact about this").
- **POV:** first person past, Sela Renn, 31, third-generation cartographer; counts, measures,
  distrusts her own eyes over the record.
- **Continuity:** Ark Meridian, gen-ship, 411 years under way, 11,000 souls. The Purpose (founding
  mission, taught as gospel); founding-day; the **drift diagram** taught in schoolrooms — *Sela drew
  the current one, in 409.* Cartographers' Office; Chart Room 2 and the master chart (3 m × 5 m
  laminate). Ch.1: Deck 14-aft, frame 90→118, chart says 161 m, true 169.11 m — a wall built 8 m
  short of the hull hiding a hatch to **Deck Nought** (old observation deck; the window; the chair
  worn smooth).
- **The secret:** the ship stopped accelerating ~91 years ago and is coasting; the Office edits the
  map generation to generation as a mercy. **Ilkka Vosse**, 86, Master Cartographer, has known 41
  years and has given Sela **the key** and a month. The unlabelled folder holds **eleven** filings of
  169 m across four centuries — one of them **Sela's grandmother's**.
- **Ch.4 (new canon):** Nine-spinward, residential, resurvey. The **Tolvers**, compartment 41: Renk
  (vats, grey hands), Mira (does the corridor's accounts in her head), **Emil**, 9, who has copied
  Sela's own drift diagram by hand — *THIS IS HOW WE KNOW WE ARE GOING SOMEWHERE* — a baby not yet
  named (naming at four months), and **Bettine Tolver**, 81, dying. **The reckoning:** half the ship
  cuts a mark on the doorframe for each child on their naming-day, with the ship's cumulative
  distance (off the founding-day proclamation) cut in beside it. Bettine asks *"how far did we get,
  in me?"* Sela does the arithmetic out loud, well, without hesitating, and gives her the number —
  and cuts the mark for the baby with the boy's hand under hers. She becomes **the twelfth** not in
  Chart Room 2 but in a doorway, for free, by being kind.
- **Ch.5 (canon):** day 14, alone, no Vosse. Deck Nought **140.04 m** of "solid hull". The recording
  sat on a plain shelf, *made to be found, not hidden*, under a plate in the First Cartographer's hand:
  **"For whoever measures true."** She is **old** in it (she lived the whole way into the lie and made
  this at the end of it). Her spine: *the drive would not hold in the 91st year; we argued a year and I
  argued for the truth and I was wrong.* **"A people does not live on where it is going. A people lives
  on believing it is going somewhere."* Take the destination and you don't get brave truth-facers — you
  get a generation that stops cutting the number into the doorframe. **"A horizon painted on the inside
  of the hull… is a road, if enough feet believe in it."** And: *"Whether to tell you is the one line I
  could not draw for you… it belongs to whoever is holding the key."* Sela's break: she had believed
  truth and kindness were one object seen from two sides — Bettine's face broke that. **The mercy that
  undid her was the key, not the painted horizon** (they didn't seal the deck). Decided nothing on day
  14 — "a thing decided in the hour you first understand its size is a flinch."
- **Ch.6 — the finale (canon; book COMPLETE 2026-07-17):**
  - **Vosse, day 19:** she takes him down (40 min; he takes her arm the last 2 m). He'd been down
    **once, at 45, for eleven minutes**, and carried the key 41 years — *"those two facts are the same
    fact, and I have known it the whole time."* Nobody had ever asked him what it was like. She asks.
    His line: *"It was like finding out the whole ship is a room with one person in it. And then
    finding out the person is you. And then having to go up and make tea."*
  - **The chair worn smooth = the payoff of a ch.1 detail.** 411 years, nobody maintains Deck Nought,
    one chair, arms polished by hundreds of hours of hands. **Eleven people polished it, each certain
    they were the only one.**
  - **The folder** is unlabelled, third drawer, **never locked** — "the founders did not build this out
    of locks; they built it out of the correct assumption that almost nobody measures a corridor twice."
    Hands are fingerprints (we're taught to copy the First Cartographer's capitals and each fail our own
    way). **The sixth is Aurelie Renn, 371, aged 34 — her grandmother** — who lived 41 more years, came
    to Sela's apprenticing, said she had "a good steady line," and never said one word.
  - **The thesis:** *"Eleven flinches, laid end to end across four centuries, look exactly like a
    policy."* What she could not accept was never the coasting or the painted horizon — it was that the
    biggest act on the ship had been done eleven times **in solitude**, and a person alone doesn't choose,
    they flinch and spend forty years calling it a decision.
  - **What she recorded / left as hull:** filed the true **169.11 m** into the folder — **the twelfth**,
    and the **first to sign it** (Sela Renn, 411, bad capitals). Then drew the **grey block herself**, by
    hand — *identical to the one before it, a completely different object: that one I inherited, this one
    I chose.* Did **not** broadcast, and the reason is hers, not the founders': *"one person, an
    afternoon, eleven thousand — that is not the truth arriving, it is a flinch with a very large
    radius."*
  - **The honest map for one person:** she charts **the room, not the hull** — Deck Nought true, the
    chair, *what the arms are worth in hours*, and **twelve names** (nine days' work off the hands and
    the Office rolls; two marked **uncertain with reasoning** — "a map that hides its error bars is a
    poster"; 6th = *my grandmother*; 11th = Ilkka Vosse, 370–411, forty-one years, eleven minutes; 12th
    = her). Left on the shelf beside the reader **under the same plate**, unsealed, no instruction.
    *The founders left an argument; an argument is one more way of being alone. She left a map — the
    whole point of a map is that somebody was here before you.* The 13th will still sit in the chair
    (the chair is the price) — but will know in the first minute they are not the first.
  - **Vosse retires**, files it himself, squares the pen, gives her the Office. At the door: *"Which did
    you file? The block, or the number?"* — **"Both."** — *"Good."* (The largest thing he ever said to
    her.)
  - **The last measurement — the design revealed:** she could close the 8 m discrepancy in an afternoon
    (renumber the frames). **Eleven keyholders never did.** Because *the grey block on the chart is the
    kind story; the eight metres in the corridor is the door* — same hand, same morning, on purpose.
    Every generation the Office **paints the sky and leaves the crack**. So she leaves it, and does not
    touch the **22-year resurvey rotation: Deck 14-aft falls in 433**, when **Emil Tolver is 31**. Final
    line of the book's argument: a map is a thing one person makes and hands to another, and the only
    question is **who you are making it for** — *"I am making it for him. I have never met him. He is
    nine."*
- **Arc:** wants a complete correct chart; needs to decide what a true map is *for* (order vs mercy).
  Landed: not truth-vs-lie but *for whom*; she ends the solitude rather than the story.
