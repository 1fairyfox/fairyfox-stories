# Book-notes — The Girl Who Sold the Wind (internal)

- **Author-persona:** Amara Okri — exuberant fabulist. **Style:** fable/folkloric, told-aloud;
  pattern of threes, turned phrases, sly asides, a sideways moral. Bright, never grim. Zari tells it
  aloud to a paying listener and keeps promising "the true telling" — the one that cost her.
- **POV:** first person past, Zari (14); boastful, funny, addresses the reader; learns hard & sweet.
- **Continuity:** the Salt Road across the Bright Waste; the Ninth Waystation (striped cloth, blue
  bottles). Winds are real, bottled, sold (dawn / grief / homesick); the **homesick wind** is the
  heaviest and costliest — it carries you home once. The Road's oldest rule: **"a wind, once sold,
  cannot be recalled."** Ch.1: Zari sells the homesick wind to a veiled stranger who pays triple in
  **wind-metal** and never haggles — the stranger is herself an **exiled wind** (a drift, not a
  face). Ch.2: Zari cheats the water-boy **Rafiq** out of his caravan ticket and he walks anyway;
  they find a whole caravan sitting down in the sand, weeping westward, in the homesick wind's
  spill.
- **Ch.3 (new canon):**
  - Zari gives the ticket back (out of vanity, not virtue); Rafiq takes it, thanks her, and walks
    beside her anyway, splitting his water.
  - **Mother Sufra** — the caravan's tale-teller, rides at the head, sells nothing. Gives the rule of
    **wind-metal:** *a wind's own voice, minted out of what it lost.* Put a coin on your tongue and
    it tells you a true piece of its story — and is **gone**. Spent is spent.
  - The three coins = three pieces (the fable's threes): **(1)** the white city of **Halvashir** at
    the river-mouth, and the beloved evening sea-wind it named nine hundred daughters for; **(2)** the
    dry autumn, the granary fire the wind carried up the streets, and the city — needing a face to
    blame — bottling its own wind at the harbour wall, carrying it into the Waste, and striking its
    name off the calendar; **(3)** what she means to do: uncork the homesick wind over Halvashir so
    the whole city longs, unbearably and forever, for the ground it is already standing on — and sits
    down in the street and does not get up.
  - **The hook Zari didn't see in her own rule:** Halvashir sold its wind, too. *They cannot recall
    it either.* No apology, no sorrow, no thousand years can put that wind back through that gate —
    which is precisely why the stranger bought the only thing that goes home whether it is welcome
    or not.
  - **The paid turn:** before the third coin, Zari *hesitates and counts* — a city in one pan, her
    last coin in the other — while Rafiq watches and does not help her cheat. She spends it. The
    hesitation is the wound.
  - Sufra's setup for ch.4: the Road allows a fool a **third bargain** — and its hook is that it
    costs "the very thing you want most." Zari says *coin*; Sufra laughs. *"You don't know what it
    is yet, girl. That's the hook."* (It is not the money. Ch.4 must land what it really is.)
- **Arc:** wants "enough" (coin/security/stall); needs to learn cleverness-without-care = greed, and
  "enough is not a number." Ch.4 the third-bargain turn (gives up what she wants most); Ch.5 she
  buys the exiled wind a true way home by understanding it — poorer, larger.
- **Status:** ch.3 of 5 written.
