# Book-notes — The Girl Who Sold the Wind (internal)

- **Author-persona:** Amara Okri — exuberant fabulist. **Style:** fable/folkloric, told-aloud;
  pattern of threes, turned phrases, sly asides, a sideways moral. Bright, never grim. Zari tells it
  aloud to a paying listener and keeps promising "the true telling" — the one that cost her.
- **POV:** first person past, Zari (14); boastful, funny, addresses the reader; learns hard & sweet.
- **Continuity:** the Salt Road across the Bright Waste; the Ninth Waystation (striped cloth, blue
  bottles). Winds are real, bottled, sold (dawn / grief / homesick); the **homesick wind** is the
  heaviest and costliest — it carries you home once. The Road's oldest rule: **"a wind, once sold,
  cannot be recalled."** Ch.1: Zari sells the homesick wind to a veiled stranger who pays triple in
  **wind-metal** and never haggles — the stranger is herself an **exiled wind** (a drift, not a
  face). Ch.2: Zari cheats the water-boy **Rafiq** out of his caravan ticket and he walks anyway;
  they find a whole caravan sitting down in the sand, weeping westward, in the homesick wind's
  spill.
- **Ch.3 (new canon):**
  - Zari gives the ticket back (out of vanity, not virtue); Rafiq takes it, thanks her, and walks
    beside her anyway, splitting his water.
  - **Mother Sufra** — the caravan's tale-teller, rides at the head, sells nothing. Gives the rule of
    **wind-metal:** *a wind's own voice, minted out of what it lost.* Put a coin on your tongue and
    it tells you a true piece of its story — and is **gone**. Spent is spent.
  - The three coins = three pieces (the fable's threes): **(1)** the white city of **Halvashir** at
    the river-mouth, and the beloved evening sea-wind it named nine hundred daughters for; **(2)** the
    dry autumn, the granary fire the wind carried up the streets, and the city — needing a face to
    blame — bottling its own wind at the harbour wall, carrying it into the Waste, and striking its
    name off the calendar; **(3)** what she means to do: uncork the homesick wind over Halvashir so
    the whole city longs, unbearably and forever, for the ground it is already standing on — and sits
    down in the street and does not get up.
  - **The hook Zari didn't see in her own rule:** Halvashir sold its wind, too. *They cannot recall
    it either.* No apology, no sorrow, no thousand years can put that wind back through that gate —
    which is precisely why the stranger bought the only thing that goes home whether it is welcome
    or not.
  - **The paid turn:** before the third coin, Zari *hesitates and counts* — a city in one pan, her
    last coin in the other — while Rafiq watches and does not help her cheat. She spends it. The
    hesitation is the wound.
  - Sufra's setup for ch.4: the Road allows a fool a **third bargain** — and its hook is that it
    costs "the very thing you want most." Zari says *coin*; Sufra laughs. *"You don't know what it
    is yet, girl. That's the hook."* (It is not the money. Ch.4 must land what it really is.)
- **Ch.4 (canon):** the third bargain. The stall won't mint — *a coin you give gladly is worth
  nothing*. What mints is **"I never wanted to be the hungry orphan again"** — the wall/store/full
  jar/never-again; *enough* was never money, it was a wall against being that child. She breaks a
  **corner** off it to buy a fast wind (3 days to Halvashir); the small lightness "felt like standing
  in a doorway with the door open and the cold coming in." **Mother Sufra's hook, stated:** at the
  gate she'll be asked for it **whole**, in front of the stranger, with no Sufra and no Rafiq to help
  — *"Do not be clever. At the gate, be **poor**. It is the only coin she will take."* Ends with the
  gate in sight and the stranger just below them.
- **Ch.5 — the finale (canon; book COMPLETE 2026-07-17):**
  - **The clever plan Zari refuses** (voiced, then dropped): shame the wind off the wall with *"do
    it and by morning you are the thing they said you were."* True, and it would work — and it wins
    by making her love a leash. Greed's shape: find what they can't bear to lose, put your hand on
    it. She shuts her mouth instead.
  - **Why the honest trade fails:** she mints the whole coin and offers it *for the bottle* — and it
    goes soft as wet salt, because the homesick wind is worth ten stalls: there's no losing in a
    trade that hands her back *enough* with change. "I had only moved my wall to the other side of
    the gate." The rule bites her one last time: **spent is spent**, and nothing had been spent.
  - **The stranger's real answer** (first honest question Zari ever asked): the homesick wind
    **will not lift her** — home was *an evening*, the hour the boats came in and eight hundred
    windows opened, and "you cannot be carried to a Tuesday." So: "a wind that cannot go home has
    one thing left it can do with a wind that goes home." Not revenge — **company**: *"I would like
    somebody to know what it is like."* She is Zari at the fire, forty years on.
  - **The payment:** *"Come home with me."* No stall, no roof, no coin — **"there's room."** Minted
    hard and spent for **nothing coming back** — the only trade on the Road with real losing in it,
    which is the one that is not a trade. The never-again went "out of me like a lamp." Never got it
    back.
  - **THE NAME — the fable's hinge (new canon):** the wind is **Shir**. The city is **Halva-shir**.
    They struck her off the calendar and kept her name in their own mouths, said twice a day for
    forty years, and not one of them **meant her by it**. And the Road's oldest rule turns on its own
    word: *a wind, once sold, cannot be **recalled*** — *called back*. Halvashir cannot recall her;
    nobody ever said anything about **calling**. Zari, poor, says "**Shir**," and means her.
  - **The ending:** the bottle drops and breaks on the gate-stones — the homesick wind loose over
    Halvashir with nobody's hand on it. It does not take the city (a city standing in its own streets
    is not homesick; the plan always required her to *put* it on them, high, deliberately — and her
    hand was empty). It finds the loudest longing within a mile and carries **Shir** home — three
    feet, to a fourteen-year-old with nothing and an open door. **Home is not a place; it is whoever
    is calling you and means it.** The city never learns; there is no thanks ("a fable that ends in
    thanks is a fable someone is selling you").
  - **Rafiq's payoff:** present at the gate, does **not** pull her out — "the hardest kind of love
    there is." Zari realises she walked a thousand miles beside the answer: she cheated him and he
    came anyway; nobody paid him; he simply wanted her along.
  - **The frame closes:** yes, she got the stall back, years later, *and it did not matter* — the
    sting and the moral, sideways: **enough was never a number.** Shir lives with her at the Ninth
    Waystation (why there's an evening sea-wind 900 miles from any sea; why her lamp won't blow out;
    she takes her name from Zari most evenings at the turn of the day). The one wind Zari never
    priced. *"That will be one copper. And no. She is not for sale."*
- **Arc:** wants "enough" (coin/security/stall); needs to learn cleverness-without-care = greed, and
  "enough is not a number." Landed: she wins by care, not cleverness; poorer and larger.
- **Status:** **COMPLETE — 5 of 5 written (2026-07-17).** Not sequel-locked → enters the eligible
  pool for the weekly sequel roll anchored 2026-07-21. (The Salt Road holds many other tales; a
