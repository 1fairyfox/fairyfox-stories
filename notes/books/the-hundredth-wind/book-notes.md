## Form fit

Novelette = one clean spine with a real second act. A three-tries fable is exactly that: commission →
road + first sight of the quarry → try one (mastery) → try two (trickery) → the vise (the Emir) →
try three (truth) → catch + hook + reckoning. Seven chapters, no B-plot. Resisted a frame (Girl was
framed; this stays unframed — distinct shape) and resisted a second quarry; one wind carries it.

## Distinctness check (vs. the shelf)

- Same realm + same author as *The Girl Who Sold the Wind* — **by the rolls**, and made distinct
  everywhere else: narrator is an **old, famous, prideful man** (vs. a hungry fourteen-year-old girl);
  engine is **confession** (vs. chase); shape is **three failed tries** (vs. pursuit-and-bargain);
  stakes are **a life's lie + every caged wind** (vs. a city in peril); the antagonist **believes he
  is mercy** (vs. an exiled wind's wrong). No cast overlap, no plot dependence — a reader needs
  neither book to enjoy the other.
- Vs. the last plant (*The Blindfold Act*, wry carny mystery): different genre, era, register, realm,
  author. Consecutive plants read as two different writers — which they are.
- The premise grows a codex seed ("the wind no one will bottle, and the child sent to catch it") —
  the realm deepening exactly the way `universe-system.md` wants.

## Author-persona (the authoring lens — Amara Okri, second book)

Fable/folkloric: cadenced, told-aloud, the pattern of threes, the sly aside, morals arriving
sideways. For this book the Okri music carries an **older, showman's ache** — Basri narrates like a
man who has told every story but one. Signature moves to keep: the turned phrase; the rule of threes
(three laws, three tries, three towns); the wink that goes quiet exactly once (ch.6 — the confession
is the book's bare-beat chapter; drop the cadence there and let it land plain). Okri's blind spot
(sweetness) is guarded by Basri's vanity — he is not sweet, he is *performing*, until he isn't.

## Voice & POV

First person, past tense, **Basri Yel**. He narrates the way he works a crowd — flourish, misdirection,
the pause before the reveal — and the reader should slowly notice the flourishes cluster wherever the
First Catch comes up. The daylight between account and truth (voice-and-pov) *is* the book: he is a
reliable narrator about winds and an unreliable one about exactly one morning forty years ago. Guard
against: I-stacking (open on the wind, the glass, the road); naming feelings (he deflects into craft
when moved — show the hands); narrator=author (Okri's warmth stays under his vanity, not on top).

## Character (want / need / flaw / agency)

- **Basri — wants:** the hundredth catch, the legend sealed. **Needs:** to say the true thing.
  **Flaw that bites:** the polished lie — it writes the false confession (ch.4), loses Noor's trust,
  summons the Emir's nets, and almost lets him walk (ch.5). **Agency:** every turn is his choice —
  taking the commission, trying mastery, trying the trick, walking away, turning back, and paying.
  The complicity beat (character.md) is ch.5: he has all the facts, the walk-away costs him nothing
  and is *paid*, the reward is real — and he takes it, and the low point is that it works. Ch.6 has
  to reverse it against a milestone, not a lecture.
- **Noor** — the foil: nothing to protect, so she can say true things at full price. Her want (to
  stand in weather once) is the book's quiet second engine — it makes the vault's stillness a wrong
  and the finale's freed storm a gift. Not wise-child-magic: her ear is *trained*, from years of
  listening to glass. She breaks trust when lied to (ch.4) and earns the trade at the end.
- **Emir Duqal** — believes he is right, and his grief is real (a storm took his son's ship). His
  method kills winds into dead air — the realm's horror, shown restrained (one small demonstration,
  no cruelty played for spectacle). He is answered, not defeated: the freed weather carries out to
  him something of the sea and the ship — not a punishment — and he opens his fists. Never a cartoon.
- **Nuru Yel** — the absent centre. The better catcher; taught him everything except the truth-telling.
  Dead or not is **never resolved** — the not-knowing is Basri's carried price. What he can restore is
  authorship: the towns now tell the First Catch with her name in it. "I could not give her back her
  life; I gave her back her catch."

## The fable mechanics (fair-play — plant everything before it pays)

- **The naming law:** every wind has one true name; speaking it into the bottle's mouth is the catch.
  The Unbottled's name is different for every catcher — the thing you will not say. (Stated ch.2 as
  trade-lore; understood ch.6; paid ch.7.)
- **The proving law:** a delivered bottle is uncorked once at the buyer's threshold to prove the wind
  is live. (Planted ch.2 in Basri's three laws; cited again in ch.4 paperwork; **sprung ch.7** — the
  hook in the bargain. The Emir's own commission language "bottled and delivered" makes it binding.)
- **The stopper:** Nuru's cracked bottle-stopper, carried forty years in the breast pocket. Planted
  ch.1 (his hand goes to it, unexplained); taken by the wind ch.3; on a doorstep among the wind's
  airings ch.4; named plainly ch.6; kept — the only thing kept — ch.7.
- **What the wind does:** opens shut things. Never malicious, never kind — it airs. Its circuit robs
  the same three towns of their secrets seasonally; after ch.3 it runs Basri's road instead. The
  escalation is *what it opens*: coats → pages → a milestone → him → the vault.
- **The white house:** the House of Still Air — windless by construction, the vault at its heart
  where ninety-nine bottles knock. Noor's whole biography. The finale's stage.

## Restraint (genre-range bar)

The forty-year-old storm and Nuru's fate: told plainly in confession, never depicted; no body, no
horror imagery — the wrong is the *running and the taking*, which is moral, not graphic. The netting
of winds is menace-by-implication (dead air, a stilled bottle) with one restrained demonstration.
Noor's captivity is neglect-shaped, not abuse-shaped, and ends in weather. Bright, propulsive,
Carmen-Sandiego danger; the darkest thing in the book is a sentence a man won't say.

## Style dials (hold every chapter)

Diction: fable-formal with desert concreteness (salt, glass, cork, canvas, camel-bell); trade terms
(catcher, stand, proving, the tip of the bottle) glossed by context. Syntax: cadenced, built to be
read aloud; the pattern of threes in sentence and scene. Rhythm: patter that swells, then a short
plain line when something true happens. Figuration: winds personified precisely (each wind does one
thing); no purple. Humor: sly, warm, at Basri's expense more than anyone's. Distance: close,
confiding, performing — with the performance thinning as the book opens him. Motif: **bottles and
names** — what's kept in glass, what's kept in a name; pays off in the title beat and the moral.
