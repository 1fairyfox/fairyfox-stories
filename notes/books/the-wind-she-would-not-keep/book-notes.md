# The Wind She Would Not Keep — book-notes (the bible)

_Planted 2026-07-24 (v0.8.0). Salt Road short story, 3 chapters, ~6,800-word cap. Sequel-in-realm to
*The Girl Who Sold the Wind* — reads standalone. This is the reference every chapter is written
against; the daily GROW task should read it before writing ch.2 or ch.3._

## Premise (one line)

Zari finally keeps the stall she always wanted, signs a fair-and-square standing order to keep it safe
forever — every wind promised to one hoarder — then must break that binding bargain, forfeiting the
stall itself, to sell a dying man one last sea-wind.

## Form & structure (short story — single effect)

- One situation, one decisive turn, one impression. **No subplot.** Small cast (Zari, Hadi, Naima; a
  glancing nod to Rafiq from book one, one line at most — not a scene).
- Three beats: **the Offer/signing → the Traveller/refusal (complicity beat) → the Breaking/forfeit +
  moral + frame-close.**
- Chapter lengths (guide, not formula): ch.1 ~2,000 · ch.2 ~1,900 · ch.3 ~2,900 (finale ~1.5×, owes the
  turn *and* the told-aloud close).

## Author-persona & style (HOLD for the whole book)

- **Yusra Adem** (`_authors/yusra-adem.md`): a ledger-keeper turned fabulist. Where Amara Okri (the two
  earlier Salt Road books) is exuberant, fireside, pattern-of-threes, sly-warm — **Adem is spare, weighed,
  wry, exact.** She hands you a receipt, and the sting is that it's correct.
- **Style dials:** plain concrete diction; short-to-medium sentences, the occasional flat aphorism;
  few images, each chosen like a careful buyer's one true detail; dry humour; **ledger / bargain /
  accounting metaphors**; warmth that arrives as a *settled account*, not a hug. Restraint is the style.
- **Deliberately unlike recent books:** last plant was *The One-O'Clock Bus* (Idris, cool magical-realism
  novella); before that *The Hundredth Wind* (Okri, exuberant fable). Adem's pared-down fable is a third
  distinct voice — and distinct from Okri *within the same realm*.

## POV & voice

- **First person, past tense, told-aloud** (all Salt Road tales are told aloud) — but in **older Zari's**
  voice: seventeen now, chastened by the first tale, drier and wiser and quieter than the boastful
  fourteen-year-old who "counted in threes." The spare Adem style and the grown-up narrator reinforce each
  other — the voice-change is *character growth*, not inconsistency. She is self-aware but keeps one live
  blind spot: she believes her caution is prudence and her honest contract is a *good* one. Let the
  daylight between what she says and what's true carry the tension.

## Main character — Zari (want vs. need vs. flaw)

- **Then (book one):** greedy, quick, boastful; wanted "enough" and the stall of her own; learned that
  cleverness without care is greed and gave up her fortune to do right.
- **Now (this book):** she *has* the stall (the old keeper left it to her — the exact book-one want,
  granted). She deals honestly and is proud of it.
- **Want:** to *keep* what she finally holds — stall, safety, good name.
- **Need:** to see that the reward became a cage of fear; that *enough* is not a number **or a
  possession**; not to *keep* wrongly (the mirror of book one's not to *sell* wrongly).
- **Flaw that bites:** she clings to her hard-won security and mistakes the clinging for wisdom — and
  mistakes an *honest* bargain for a *good* one. Hadi sells to exactly that flaw.
- **Agency:** every turn is her choice — she signs (ch.1), she refuses Naima (ch.2), she breaks the order
  and pays the forfeit (ch.3). The plot is her decisions and their price.

## Cast

- **Hadi Sarraf** (`_characters/hadi-sarraf.md`) — the wind-broker of the still city of **Sabr**; buys
  winds, never sells them on, until Sabr's sky hangs windless. Never lies, never cheats; contracts square
  to the coin, forfeits read aloud. Not a villain — Zari's own first-tale lesson (*a wind in the wrong
  hands is a catastrophe*) walked one step too far. Backstory (release in small pieces, never dump): a
  storm-wind took his caravan and his people in one afternoon when he was young; he has bought the weather
  up ever since so it can never take anyone again. He believes stillness is mercy. Quiet, courteous,
  unhurried; never raises his voice.
- **Naima** (`_characters/naima.md`) — eleven; walked her dying grandfather (a coast fisherman, once) a
  hundred days across the waste for the **sea-wind**, to let him smell the sea one last time. Has the
  *exact* fare, counted till she knows the coins by their edges. Doesn't argue cleverly — just needs,
  plainly, and waits. The fare the standing order cannot pay.
- **Zari** (`_characters/zari.md`) — reuse; update is authorial, not a page rewrite.
- **Rafiq** — book-one caravan boy; **one fond line at most** (e.g. his caravan is among those the
  windless season would strand, or a remembered aside). NOT a subplot.

## Salt Road canon to honour (`_universes/the-salt-road.md`)

- Winds are *things*, bottled in tall blue bottles and sold at waystations; each wind does one true thing.
- **A bargain, once made, is binding** (the engine of book one; the engine of the forfeit here). **A
  bottled wind, once sold, cannot be recalled.** Names have weight; the third of anything matters.
- Bright, not grim: danger and hard lessons, punished with a wink, not a wound. No gore, no despair.
- Texture: salt-white glare, blue bottles, camel bells, cardamom, striped cloth.
- **New seed annotated in the codex** this plant: the hoarder-broker + the seller who breaks a binding
  bargain (grew into this book).

## The engine (the fable turn)

- **The standing order** = every wind Zari stocks, triple price, forever, sold to no one else.
- **The forfeit clause** (written plainly, read aloud in ch.1 — Chekhov): *sell one bottle to another and
  the whole Ninth Waystation reverts to Sabr.* Hadi wrote patience into it; the whole stall for one bottle.
- **The Chekhov bottle:** the **sea-wind** must be seen on the shelf in ch.1 (named, in passing) so ch.2's
  ask lands as inevitability, not coincidence.
- **The complicity beat (ch.2):** Zari has every fact, the reward is real (full coffer, safe stall), and
  she refuses Naima anyway — and it costs her *nothing*. That is the horror; no monster needed
  (`craft/character.md`).
- **The turn (ch.3):** not out-tricking Hadi — *paying his fair forfeit herself.* She sells the sea-wind
  for one coin and hands over the stall. Care over cleverness — the book-one growth, at a higher price.
- **Salt Road story-logic payoff:** the winds will not *keep* for a man who cages them — Sabr's shelves
  stand full and silent and worthless (dead air), the point being made without a fight or a wound.

## Chapter map + per-chapter briefs

1. **The Standing Order** — *(WRITTEN, ship at plant.)* The Ninth Waystation now hers (old keeper's
   bequest); the fear under the good fortune (she checks the bolts, dreads the empty-shelf day); the
   sea-wind seen on the shelf in passing; Hadi Sarraf arrives from Sabr, makes his fair, reasonable,
   almost kind case; the forfeit clause read aloud; Zari signs and talks herself past the one small
   wrongness. End on the signed order and the promised shelves.
2. **The Sea-Wind** — *(GROW next.)* A quiet day; a caravan passes (Rafiq's, maybe — one line). Then
   Naima and her grandfather; the coins counted on the counter; the sea-wind bottle right there, now
   promised; Zari refuses, correctly, by contract — the **complicity beat**, and it costs her nothing,
   and she can't unsee it. She watches them walk back out into the glare. End on Zari alone with a full
   shelf and a wrong she can't name to herself yet. **New canon to honour from ch.1:** the exact terms
   of the order, the forfeit clause wording, Hadi's manner and his one small tell of grief, the state of
   Zari's fear.
3. **The Whole Stall for One Bottle** — *(GROW last; ~1.5×.)* Zari breaks the order — pays the forfeit,
   not a trick; sells Naima the sea-wind for one coin; hands the Ninth Waystation to Sabr. The grandfather
   gets his sea (gentle, not shown dying). The winds won't keep for Hadi — Sabr's shelves, full and
   silent. The moral, sideways, Adem's ledger voice; the told-aloud frame closes. She walks out with
   nothing but the open road, poorer and larger — again, and knowingly this time.

## Continuity ledger (fill as chapters land)

- **Ch.1 — canon set (ship 2026-07-24):**
  - The stall came to Zari by **bequest**, not bargain: **old Bostan** kept the Ninth Waystation forty
    years, no heir, left it to the girl who'd once "done a costly, foolish, right thing at his counter."
    Zari hung the striped (blue-and-white, noon-faded) cloth herself.
  - **Zari's fear made concrete:** she counts the bottles twice a day, checks the deed (folded in the
    **tea-tin**), and dreads a **windless season** — the shelves emptying, the stall standing over dust
    and a deed. She has watched two waystations close that way. This is the fear Hadi names.
  - **Hadi Sarraf of Sabr:** arrives on a **still day**, his caravan carrying a dead calm (no bell
    rings). Never lies; contract drawn already-written, read aloud in full, genuinely **fair** ("not
    crooked, but square"). His **grief-tell:** a flat place crosses his face where a grief has been
    walked over so long the ground no longer feels it — "A wind has no malice … one true thing, in the
    one wrong hand, at the one wrong hour." He calls his hoarding **mercy**, "and I sleep."
  - **The standing order (exact terms):** every wind Zari stocks, **triple** waystation price, forever,
    for as long as she keeps the stall; he replaces each sold bottle before the gap shows — **on the one
    condition she sells to no one but Sabr.**
  - **The forfeit clause (exact wording to honour):** "should the keeper sell so much as a single bottle
    to any hand but Sabr's, the Ninth Waystation, its cloth, its shelves, and its deed, revert entire to
    the buyer. In full. For that one bottle." Hadi: "I have written it hard so that you will keep it
    easy."
  - **The Chekhov bottle:** the **sea-wind** is the **third bottle on the high shelf** — a grey salt
    breath of a coast, bought years ago off a dying caravan-master, never once sold (no sea within reach).
    Zari's eye goes to it as the forfeit is read; she thinks *I'll never sell that one anyway.*
  - **The signing:** Zari **felt the wrongness** (the old watchful stillness she'd learned as a child to
    trust) and **overruled it, calling it "prudence."** Signs in her own steady hand. Hadi dries the ink
    by **blowing on it — the only wind he permits.** Ends: shelves full, all promised, Zari feels *safe*
    ("safe as a held breath").
  - **Frame:** told-aloud, first person, older Zari, to a listener; she flags "prudence" as the word that
    "will not be worth what I paid for it."
  - **Timing hook into ch.2:** Naima arrives **three days later** ("she had counted her coins so many
    times across the waste that she knew them by their edges in the dark").

## Pitfalls to avoid (this book specifically)

- Don't let Hadi become a cartoon miser — his mercy must be sincere and his contracts genuinely fair.
- Don't solve ch.3 with cleverness (that would repeat neither the fable's promise nor Zari's growth) —
  she must *pay*, plainly.
- Keep the grandfather's death off-page and gentle; the register is bright/propulsive, not grief-soaked
  (`genre-range.md`). Make it gripping and warm before it is sad.
- Hold Adem's spare style — resist Okri's exuberance and threes; this teller is drier.
- No infodump of Hadi's backstory — release it in small pieces (a line in ch.1, more in ch.3).
