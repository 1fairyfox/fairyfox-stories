# Character — real, flawed, and driving the story

Characters are the reason readers stay. In this project the main character is **first person**
and the book generally follows them; that intimacy only works if the person on the page feels
*real*. This note is a standing constraint, not a suggestion.

## The non-negotiables

- **A real character, never a hollow vessel.** The idea that the protagonist should be a
  blank everyman so the reader can "project" onto them is **disproven** in writing communities
  — a flat, faultless lead drains the conflict and the connection. Readers bond with
  *specific, flawed* people, not empty avatars ([The Mary Sue](https://www.themarysue.com/what-wrong-with-self-insert-mary-sue-character/),
  [TCK Publishing](https://www.tckpublishing.com/what-is-a-mary-sue/)).
- **No Mary Sue / Gary Stu.** A character free of weaknesses, effortlessly gifted, whom
  nothing bad ever truly touches, is a wish-fulfilment doll. It kills tension: if the lead
  can't fail, the reader has nothing to fear for. The tell-tale sign — *nothing bad happens
  to them; they always escape unharmed* ([TCK](https://www.tckpublishing.com/what-is-a-mary-sue/)).
- **Agency drives plot.** A story is a chain of **consequences the character causes**, not a
  series of events that happen to them. Their choices — and the fallout — are the plot.

## Building someone real

- **Want vs. need.** A conscious external *want* (the goal that drives the plot) and a deeper,
  often unseen *need* (what would actually make them whole). The gap between them is the arc.
- **A flaw that costs them.** A real limitation — fear, pride, a wound, a blind spot — that
  makes things worse at the wrong moment. Flaws must *bite*, not be cosmetic ("clumsy but
  gorgeous" is not a flaw).
- **Contradiction.** Real people hold opposing traits (tender and cruel, brave and evasive).
  One well-placed contradiction makes a character feel three-dimensional.
- **Specificity.** Concrete habits, objects, ways of noticing the world. A named, particular
  detail beats a paragraph of adjectives.
- **Change (or its refusal).** By the end they are altered by what happened — grown, broken,
  or knowingly refusing to change. A lead who ends exactly as they began wasted the reader's
  time (unless that stasis *is* the tragic point, made to land).

## Consequence is the engine

Give the protagonist something they want badly enough to act, then make them **pay** for
their choices; each decision should ratchet the tension up, not reset it
([Palmetto](https://www.palmettopublishing.com/blog/writing/elements-of-great-short-story),
[Spines](https://spines.com/short-story-elements/)). Danger the lead always shrugs off is
the Mary Sue trap in slow motion.

## The supporting cast

- **Everyone wants something.** Even a one-scene character has an agenda; that's what makes
  them feel alive rather than furniture.
- **No cardboard antagonist.** The opposing force believes it is right. The best antagonists
  are the protagonist's argument turned against them.
- **Foils sharpen.** A supporting character who embodies the choice the lead won't make
  throws the lead's arc into relief.

## Quick self-check (every book)

1. Does the lead have a flaw that actually causes damage in the story?
2. Do their choices cause the major turns (agency), or are they a passenger?
3. Could any reader mistake them for a flawless self-insert? If so, wound them, complicate
   them, let them fail.
4. Does the cast each want something, including the antagonist?
5. Are they *specific* — could you pick their voice out of a line-up?
