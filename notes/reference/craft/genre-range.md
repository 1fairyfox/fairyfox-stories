# Genre & range — keeping the shelf wide

The shelf must feel **varied and alive** — every book its own premise, voice, energy, and
genre. This note keeps the farm from drifting into a house style or a rut. Range is a
first-class quality goal, not decoration.

## The mandate

- **Wide coverage.** Across the shelf: literary, mystery, thriller, horror, science fiction,
  fantasy (low and high), historical, contemporary/slice-of-life, romance, adventure, magical
  realism, satire/comedy, coming-of-age, noir, speculative/weird, and blends of these.
- **Vary the everything.** Tone (cosy ↔ harrowing), scope (a single room ↔ a world), energy
  (quiet ↔ propulsive), time period, setting, and the *kind* of protagonist. Two adjacent
  books should never feel like siblings.
- **Sometimes swing.** Deliberately reach for the creative / out-of-the-box — an unusual
  premise, an odd structure, an unexpected narrator, a concept that shouldn't work and does.
  Not every book, but the shelf should surprise.

## Guardrails so range never lowers the bar

- **Range widens *what*, never lowers *how*.** A comic satire and a bleak horror are both held
  to `what-makes-fiction-good.md`. Experimental is not an excuse for sloppy.
- **First person + a real lead still holds** across every genre.
- **Match form to premise** (`forms.md`) — a sprawling epic idea probably isn't a short story;
  a single-image idea isn't a novel.
- **Distinctness check before planting:** scan `stories/` and the pitched ideas — is this
  genuinely different in premise, voice, and shape from what's already on the shelf? If it
  echoes an existing book, change it or pick another.

## Anti-sameness practice

- Rotate genres and tones deliberately from book to book; don't plant three quiet literary
  pieces in a row.
- Vary narrator type (age, temperament, era, reliability) and setting.
- Keep a short **idea bench** in `notes/plans/` — pitched premises across genres — and pull
  from it to force breadth, while still rolling the universe (`universe-system.md`) and form.

## Content range & consent

Books may go dark or intense. That's allowed and can be excellent — but the **blueprint**
(open, spoiler-forward) and a quiet **content note** on the book give informed consent up
front. Range never means gratuitous; intensity must serve the story.
