# Serialization — writing a book a chapter at a time

Books here are **grown a chapter at a time** (up to 5 books advanced per day, one whole
chapter each). The book is fully blueprinted up front, but it's *revealed* serially — so each
chapter must satisfy on its own and pull to the next. Sources:
[MasterClass — serialized](https://www.masterclass.com/articles/how-to-write-a-serialized-story),
[Wattpad — serialised fiction](https://www.wattpad.com/332437209-how-to-write-serialised-fiction-how-to-structure),
[Indie Author Magazine](https://indieauthormagazine.com/the-intentional-craft-of-successful-serialized-fiction/).

## The serial chapter's double job

Each chapter must **stand and connect**:

- **Stand:** a self-contained beat with its own small arc — a scene resolves, a question
  answers, something *happens*. A chapter that's all setup and no payoff frustrates a serial
  reader.
- **Connect:** it ends on pull — a turn, a new question, a rising complication that ties
  directly into the next chapter's opening tension. Not every ending is a cliffhanger, but
  every ending is a reason to come back.

## Because it's blueprinted first

- **No pantsing drift.** The blueprint already knows the arc and each chapter's job, so serial
  release never paints the book into a corner (the classic web-serial failure).
- **The plan can flex, not lurch.** A chapter may run long or short, split, or merge with its
  neighbour as the prose demands — but the destination is fixed. Update the blueprint if a
  chapter's shape genuinely changes; keep it honest to what's written.

## Writing one whole chapter at a time

- **One chapter per session, but a *finished* one.** If a chapter doesn't fit a single
  generation output, complete it across segments and stitch — never ship half a chapter.
- **Continuity is sacred.** Re-read the previous chapter (and the book-notes) before writing
  the next: names, established facts, timeline, the narrator's current state and knowledge.
  A serial lives or dies on consistency.
- **Hold the voice.** The first-person voice must be identical from chapter 1 to the last —
  the book-notes `voice.md` is the reference.

## Pacing across the serial

- **Track the arc position.** Know where this chapter sits (setup / rising / midpoint / climb
  / climax / fall / resolution) and pace it accordingly; don't release all the tension early,
  and don't stall.
- **Mini-arcs inside the macro-arc.** Group chapters into the form's larger movements; a
  novella's ~12 chapters still climb one hill.

## Per-chapter checklist

1. Did I re-read the prior chapter + book-notes for continuity and voice?
2. Does this chapter *stand* (its own small arc, something happens)?
3. Does it *connect* (ends on real pull into the next)?
4. Is it a whole, finished chapter — assembled fully if it took multiple segments?
5. Does the blueprint still match what I wrote (or did I update it honestly)?
