# Structure — how a book is built

Form sets the *scale* of structure (`forms.md`); this note is the shared architecture beneath
all of them. Sources: [Palmetto](https://www.palmettopublishing.com/blog/writing/elements-of-great-short-story),
[Behind the Draft](https://behindthedraft.com/how-to-write-a-perfect-short-story/),
[MasterClass — serialized](https://www.masterclass.com/articles/how-to-write-a-serialized-story).

## The core loop (the atom of engagement)

*Vivid situation → a pressure or choice that matters → the character commits → the
consequence lands → a new question opens.* The reader is pulled by **curiosity + stakes** —
wanting to know what happens, and caring what it costs. Every scene should run this loop.

## The arc

The classic spine — **exposition → rising action → climax → falling action → resolution** —
scaled to the form:

- **Short story:** the whole arc, compressed; one turn is the climax.
- **Novelette/novella:** a clear inciting incident, a genuine rising middle, a climax that
  pays off the central conflict, a resolution that lands. The novella keeps *one* arc, deep.
- **Novel:** multiple arcs braided — a midpoint shift, escalating complications, a low point
  before the climax, threads converging, subplots resolving into the theme.

Plan the arc in the blueprint before writing. Know the ending before chapter one — you write
*toward* something.

## Scene & sequel

A useful unit beneath the chapter:

- **Scene** — goal → conflict/obstacle → outcome (usually a setback or a costly win). This is
  where tension lives.
- **Sequel** — the character's reaction → dilemma → decision, which sets the next scene's goal.
  This is where meaning and causation live.

Alternating them keeps a book *causal* (each beat because of the last) instead of episodic.

## Chapters

- A chapter is a **unit of change and pull**: it advances a thread *and* ends on a reason to
  continue (a turn, a question, a shift — not necessarily a cheap cliffhanger).
- **Length is a craft call, never a formula.** A chapter may be a few paragraphs or many
  pages; never divide word-count by a chapter target. Let the scene decide.
- In serialized growth (a chapter written per day), each chapter should also read as a
  **self-contained beat with a small arc**, while feeding the larger one (`serialization.md`).

## Pacing

- **Rising stakes:** each turn a little higher than the last.
- **Breathe before the turn:** a quiet beat makes the reveal hit; relentless intensity flattens.
- **Vary the rhythm:** summary to cover distance, scene to slow into what matters. Control of
  distance is control of pace.

## Openings & endings

- **Open in motion**, tension already present; hook within the first beat.
- **End earned:** the resolution traces back to the character's choices — inevitable in
  hindsight, not arbitrary. No cut-to-black for lack of a real ending.

## Blueprint checklist

1. Do I know the ending, and does every chapter aim at it?
2. Is the arc scaled correctly to the form?
3. Is the book causal (scene→sequel), not a list of events?
4. Does each planned chapter change something and pull forward?
5. Where are the breaths before the big turns?
