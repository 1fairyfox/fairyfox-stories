# Voice & POV — first person, done well

Every book here is **first person**, generally following one main character. First person is
intimate and immediate — and unusually easy to do badly. This note is the guardrail.
Sources: [Bookfox](https://thejohnfox.com/2016/06/writing-in-first-person/),
[Jane Friedman](https://janefriedman.com/the-peril-and-promise-of-writing-in-first-person-pov/),
[Writing Excuses 12.2](https://writingexcuses.com/12-2-how-to-nail-character-voice-in-first-person/).

## Build a genuine interior voice

- **A person, not a camera.** The narrator has specific opinions, a particular way of
  noticing, recognizable preoccupations. The *way they narrate* — word choice, rhythm, what
  they fixate on and what they skip — is characterization. Two of our narrators should never
  sound alike.
- **Interiority is the payoff.** First person buys us the inside of a head — use it: judgment,
  association, self-deception. The internal voice should be as vivid as the dialogue.
- **The voice reflects who they are.** A grief-numb narrator and a giddy one describe the same
  room in different words. Let the state bleed into the prose.

## The pitfalls (avoid every one)

- **"I"-stacking.** Sentence after sentence opening with *I* — prose-killing. Vary the entry:
  start on the object, the sensation, the world.
- **Emotional telling.** First person tempts you to *state* feelings ("I was terrified").
  Render them instead — through what the narrator does, notices, and misreads. Show the racing
  detail, not the label.
- **Claustrophobia / lost physicality.** Trapped in the head, pacing dies and the body
  vanishes. Keep the narrator *in a place, doing things*; break up interiority with action and
  sense.
- **The over-reliable narrator.** A voice so accurate and self-aware that the reader never
  knows more than they do removes tension. Let them misjudge, withhold, rationalize — the gap
  between what they say and what's true is a gift.
- **Narrator = author.** The narrator is not you. Guard against defaulting to your own cadence,
  tastes, and reflexes; give them theirs.
- **Perspective breaks.** You cannot state another character's private thoughts — only what the
  narrator observes and infers. No head-hopping.

## Tense

Second-person-adjacent immediacy isn't required, but choose a tense on purpose. Past tense is
the flexible default; present tense heightens immediacy and urgency (good for tense,
close-focus books) but is relentless — pick per book and hold it consistently.

## Quick self-check

1. Could you identify this narrator from a single unattributed paragraph?
2. Are feelings *rendered* (action/detail) rather than *named*?
3. Is there daylight between the narrator's account and the truth (tension)?
4. Have you varied sentence openings away from "I"?
5. Does the body/place stay present, or has it dissolved into a monologue?
