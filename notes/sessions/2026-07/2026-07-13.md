### What was written

- **The Wintering House — ch.4 "The Family Piece."** Closes the "Comfortable Lie" movement. Six
  weeks of Agnes's liturgy in the warm room, which she loves; Charlotte gives her the dead
  mistress's shawl ("it's nobody's… she'd want it used") and Agnes prefers the shawl to the
  question. At two in the morning she takes a candle to the family portrait on the stair and finds
  three companions — Miss Pargeter, Miss Vane, Miss Loder — painted in afterwards, in three
  different hands, behind the mistress's chair; and beside the last of them a patch of canvas sized,
  primed and waiting. Mrs Hale: *"It don't get bigger. It's always been one."* At breakfast the
  family ask her to sit for the painter. She says yes, knowing exactly what she is saying yes to,
  and goes upstairs and cries with happiness. The paid turn is the flaw doing what the flaw does —
  she folds the cold clear part of herself away, and it costs her nothing to do it.
- **The Cartographer of Decks — ch.4 "The Weight of a True Map."** Sela carries Vosse's key for
  twelve days without using it, then draws a residential resurvey on Nine-spinward. The Tolvers give
  her the good tea they can't afford; nine-year-old Emil shows her his hand-copy of the drift diagram
  — *the diagram Sela herself drew for the schools in 409* — and the doorframe **reckoning**, where
  each child's naming-mark is cut beside the ship's cumulative distance. Dying Bettine Tolver asks
  *"how far did we get, in me?"* Sela does the arithmetic out loud, well, without a tremor, gives her
  the beautiful false number, and then cuts the new baby's mark with the boy's hand under hers. She
  becomes the twelfth keeper of the secret not in Chart Room 2 and not by deciding — but in a
  doorway, for free, by being kind for four hours and being very good at it.
- **The Girl Who Sold the Wind — ch.3 "The Wind's Story."** Zari gives Rafiq's ticket back out of
  vanity, not virtue; he takes it, thanks her, and walks beside her anyway, splitting his water.
  Mother Sufra gives the rule of wind-metal — *a wind's own voice, minted out of what it lost;* spend
  a coin and it tells you one true piece of its story, and is gone. The fable's threes land as three
  spent coins: the white city of **Halvashir** and the evening sea-wind it named nine hundred
  daughters for; the granary fire the wind carried up the streets, and the city bottling its own wind
  and casting it into the Waste for want of a face to blame; and what the exiled wind means to do —
  uncork the homesick wind over Halvashir so a whole city longs, forever, for the ground it is
  standing on. The paid turn: before the third coin Zari **hesitates and counts**, a city in one pan
  and her last coin in the other, while Rafiq watches and does not help her cheat. Sufra sets the
  third bargain: it will cost her the thing she wants most. Zari says *coin*. Sufra laughs.

### Craft note learned

Two of the three chapters land their turn on a character being **kind, or happy, and knowing better**
— Agnes says yes to the portrait; Sela gives a dying woman a false number and is good at it. Added to
`craft/common-pitfalls.md`: the strongest complicity beat isn't a character being fooled, it's a
character *doing the fooling to themselves* and finding it costs nothing. Terror-free, restraint-safe,
and much harder to look away from.
