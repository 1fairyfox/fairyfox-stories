## Written — one whole chapter each

- **The Two-O'Clock Launderette — ch.3 "Fenn Street, 2 a.m." → THE BOOK IS COMPLETE (3/3).** The
  hollow hour ends on the first page and the weight comes *back* into the paper — the magic showed
  Nadia ninety seconds of what unburdened feels like, the way you hold someone's bag at a bus stop so
  they know what it weighs, and then handed it back. So she cleans. Beautifully. For three and a half
  hours, because cleaning is what you do instead of telephoning. She nearly rings at half two and
  stops — you cannot ring a seventy-one-year-old man at half two in the morning; there is exactly one
  thing that call means, and it's the call they got about her mum. She makes a deal with herself for
  six o'clock, keeps it on the step of the shuttered shop in the blue light, and her dad answers on
  the fourth ring and sounds *exactly the same*. "Is everything all right?" — "No. But I thought I'd
  ring." The silence that follows is the chapter: he'd been sitting on his side of it with his own bit
  of paper. *I'll put the heating on.* She bins the biro that has never worked, keeps the hair
  elastic, gets the 43, and says thank you to a launderette in front of a man eating a sausage roll.
  No fix. One phone call and a bus. Exactly the ending the blueprint promised — earned, not granted.
- **The Cinderwick Job — ch.4 "The Second Thief."** Juno goes under Gallow Row for the Cage's
  drawings and finds the Draughtsman has already sold the only set — Sunday last, to a pleasant young
  man who paid in gold. Pell put the job to her on the *Monday*. **Kit Sorrel was hired before she
  was**, which means Crane never wanted a rival: he wanted her a *partner*, and didn't ask either of
  them. She finds Kit in the back room of the Fetters with the stolen drawings spread on a public
  table, waiting to be caught at it. He's brilliant — he's read the *mendings*, found the 1847 filled
  passage nine feet behind the consignment clerk — and he blows himself in one line by claiming a
  knock on a wall in a room whose clerk never leaves it. She asks for his chit. **Two-fifteen.** Hers
  is two-fourteen: same sheet, same ink, same careful hand, printed together and dropped at opposite
  ends of one small city "the way you drop two dogs in a yard and go in to your dinner." She takes his
  hand knowing every part of it, because a trap you can see is still, in every respect that matters,
  a door — and Crane is *afraid of the bird*.
- **Every Lock but Hers — ch.3 "Sully's Keys."** Eleven days of being careful (which is a job, not an
  absence), and then Mar says one word. Sully Ilesanmi, 78, retired signalman, locked out of Harrowgate
  Court for the eleventh time. The comedy of the hiding places is a list Nell has kept and shown
  nobody — the egg box, the carriage clock, a rolled sock in a drawer of socks, *under* the tin of the
  biscuit tin. Tonight she can't find them, and then she looks at the coat hooks: two of them, and the
  navy one isn't his, and has a scarf still tucked in the collar the way you tuck a scarf when you're
  coming straight back. Keys in the pocket. And Sully — braver than she is — doesn't bluster. *"You
  believe I am going soft. It is not what is happening."* A key gets **lost**; a key is **hidden**, and
  the gift of hiding a thing from yourself is that you get to have done it without having to say why.
  *"I do not want to come home to a flat where nobody is going to be at the door. So I arrange, without
  deciding, for the door not to open. And then somebody comes. It costs sixty pounds. It is the
  cheapest thing in my life."* He has no idea he is describing her. She says nothing — she's busy
  holding a door shut with her shoulder — and then accepts a cup of tea for the first time in a year,
  which is the hardest work she does all week. Home at three: the true thing sitting behind her teeth,
  the certainty that saying it would open the door onto somewhere else, and she pays nothing and gets
  nothing and the door opens onto her flat. "That's what doors do, when you don't pay." Bolts it top
  and bottom. *Every lock in that flat was on the inside. I'd fitted every one of them myself.*
