## Written — one whole chapter each

- **The Blindfold Act — ch.2 "A Cold Reading"** (~2,510 words; blueprint ch.2). Opens on the trade
  distinction that is the book's thesis: a *hot* reading is a purchase, a **cold** one is saying the true
  thing *before you've decided whether you want it to be true* — and Sonora is the best alive at it, and
  this is the chapter where she won't. The sheriff walks Bruno to the fender at nine in the morning in
  front of everybody (the point of nine in the morning); Bruno answers everything truthfully and the
  sheriff writes down nothing, "because he was not gathering; he was *fitting*." Dell the patch never
  sells him — he says *the show is clean and the show is leaving*, and the man who needs a name looks at
  the man who isn't quite inside either: **"Nobody sold Bruno. We simply all stood in a circle and left
  him outside it, which is the same, and costs less, and lets you sleep."** The code's underside gets
  named — *with it and for it* is a real love **and** a wall, and every good thing about the show was that
  week "a machine for not looking," with Sonora as the finest instrument it ever grew. The circle opens:
  Sallow cleared, **Perro** the fire-eater's debt planted (ch.3's false lead), **Ynez** written down and
  crossed out. **The tell lands small and deniable:** Dell says "He'd want us to roll," Lark's knife
  stops, her thumb lifts off the loaf, and she looks at *the stove* — and Sonora clocks it and supplies
  the innocent reading herself, because "a good reading does not come with a receipt."
  **The paid turn** (per `serialization.md`: the ending is the flaw doing what the flaw does): Lark comes
  to the wagon and gets six words out — *"Sonora. If a person —"* — and Sonora, who **knows** what's
  coming and is terrified of it, does the act instead of listening, and hands the girl back the wall she
  built her. *"She said, 'No. It's nothing.' She said it in my voice."* Withholds nothing; ends on a door
  Sonora shut herself, at the cost of four chapters and everything Lark has. First use of **Ada Prewitt**
  in the prose, once, in self-reproach.
- **The Girl Who Sold the Wind — ch.5 "The Homesick Wind"** (~3,310 words; blueprint ch.5). **The book
  ends.** Zari voices the clever plan she came a thousand miles with — shame Shir off the wall with *"do
  it and by morning you are the thing they said you were"* — and shuts her mouth, because it wins by
  making a widow's love into a leash, which is the shape of every trade she ever made. Then the honest
  trade **fails**: she mints the whole never-again coin and offers it *for the bottle*, and it goes soft
  as wet salt, because the homesick wind is worth ten stalls — *"I had only moved my wall to the other
  side of the gate."* Ch.4's rule bites her one last time: spent is spent, and nothing had been spent.
  The first honest question of her life gets the real answer: the wind **will not lift** the stranger,
  because home was *an evening* and "you cannot be carried to a Tuesday" — so it was never revenge, it
  was **company**: *"I would like somebody to know what it is like."* Zari pays by offering the one thing
  with no return in it — *"Come home with me… there's room"* — and this time the coin mints hard, because
  there is real losing in it. **The hinge (new canon):** the wind is **Shir**; the city is
  **Halva-shir**; they struck her off the calendar and kept her name in their own mouths twice a day for
  forty years and never once *meant* her by it — and the Road's oldest rule turns on its own word:
  *a wind, once sold, cannot be **recalled*** — *called back*. Halvashir cannot recall her. Nobody ever
  said anything about **calling**. The bottle breaks at the gate, the homesick wind comes out loose over
  a hundred thousand people, and it doesn't take the city (a city standing in its own streets isn't
  homesick — the plan always needed her to *put* it on them, and her hand was empty). It finds the
  loudest longing in a mile and carries Shir home: three feet, to a girl with nothing. **Home is not a
  place; it is whoever is calling you and means it.** Rafiq is there and does **not** pull her out, per
  Sufra. The city never learns; there is no thanks — *"a fable that ends in thanks is a fable someone is
  selling you."* The frame closes on the moral sideways: she got the stall back years later **and it did
  not matter** — *enough was never a number.*
- **The Cartographer of Decks — ch.6 "The Chart She Draws"** (~2,820 words; blueprint ch.6). **The book
  ends.** She takes Vosse down on day 19 — forty minutes, and he takes her arm the last two metres. He'd
  been down **once, at forty-five, for eleven minutes**, and carried the key forty-one years: *"those two
  facts are the same fact, and I have known it the whole time."* Nobody had ever asked him what it was
  like; she asks. **The chair worn smooth pays off** a ch.1 detail she'd filed as an observation about
  materials: 411 years, no maintenance, one chair, arms polished to a shine that takes hundreds of hours
  of hands — **eleven people, each certain they were the only one.** The folder was never locked ("the
  founders did not build this out of locks; they built it out of the correct assumption that almost
  nobody measures a corridor twice"); the sixth filing is **Aurelie Renn, 371, aged 34** — her
  grandmother, who lived forty-one more years, came to her apprenticing, told her she had a good steady
  line, and never said one word. The thesis: **"Eleven flinches, laid end to end across four centuries,
  look exactly like a policy."** What she couldn't accept was never the coasting or the painted horizon —
  it was that the biggest act on the ship had been performed eleven times *in solitude*, and a person
  alone doesn't choose, they flinch and spend forty years calling it a decision. **What she recorded and
  what she left as hull:** filed the true 169.11 m — the twelfth, and the first to **sign** it — then
  drew the grey block **herself**, by hand, *identical to the one before it and a completely different
  object: "that one I inherited, this one I chose."* Did not broadcast, for her own reason and not the
  founders': *"one person, an afternoon, eleven thousand — that is not the truth arriving, it is a flinch
  with a very large radius."* **The honest map for one person:** she charts the room, not the hull — the
  chair, what its arms are worth in hours, and **twelve names** (two marked uncertain with reasoning:
  "a map that hides its error bars is a poster"), left on the shelf under the founders' own plate. *They
  left an argument; an argument is one more way of being alone. She left a map — the whole point of a map
  is that somebody was here before you.* Vosse retires on **"Both."** And the design lands last: the grey
  block is the kind story, **the eight metres in the corridor is the door**, same hand, same morning, on
  purpose — every generation the Office paints the sky and leaves the crack. So she leaves it, and does
  not touch the 22-year rotation: **Deck 14-aft falls in 433, when Emil Tolver is 31.** *"I am making it
  for him. I have never met him. He is nine."*

## Maintenance — a sixth integrity check (the sequel-lock's consuming half, part (a))

`scripts/check-stories.test.mjs` gained **`book state matches chapters written, and sequel-lock is
honoured at completion`**. The existing check was one-directional — it caught `complete` with chapters
missing, but **not** a book that quietly stays `growing` after its last chapter lands. That book would
sit in the grow pool forever and never reach the sequel pool, silently, and today was the first run that
could have produced one (two finales at once). The new check derives state from the chapters on disk:
0 → `planned`, partial → `growing`, full → `complete` **or**, if `sequelLock: true`, **`complete-no-sequel`
and nothing else** — and `complete-no-sequel` may never appear without the lock.

That is **half of standing item (2)** — the flagged consuming half, part (a) — now wired and enforced,
rather than a hand-promise the daily job might forget on a run where a locked book finishes. It is a
check, not a mutation: it can't set the state, but it makes shipping the wrong one impossible. **Part (b)
— excluding `sequelLock: true` books from the weekly roll — is still owed**, and now has a deadline
(07-21). It stays flagged rather than invented, because it's a change to the roll's semantics in
`operating-model.md`, not a bug.

## Craft — learned this run

`craft/forms.md` gained **"Budget the finale, not just the chapters."** *Girl* finished at ~10,200 against
a ~9,200 word-pick — the entire overrun in ch.5, because a **framed** book owes two endings: the tale's
and the telling's, and the frame-close is where the moral goes sideways instead of becoming a lecture.
*Cartographer* came in at ~11,200 against ~11,800 with the same finale shape and no strain, because its
pick had room. New rule at planting: **cost the last chapter at ~1.5× a middle chapter**, more if the
book is framed, has a coda, or resolves through understanding rather than event.

**The overrun is recorded, not fudged.** *Girl* at ~10,200 is comfortably inside the novelette band
(7,500–17,500), so the prose stands, the delivered length is stated plainly at the top of the public
blueprint, and **`wordCap` stays at 9,200 as the honest record of the planting roll** — the audit trail
is worth more than a tidy number, and editing a roll to match its result is exactly the fudge
`operating-model.md` forbids. (`wordCap` is a planning constant; it is not surfaced on the site.)
