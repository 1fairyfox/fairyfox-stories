**Form:** novella (~28,264-word cap) · **Genre:** magical realism · **POV:** first person, past
tense · **Universe:** the Hollow Hours · **Author:** Del Marsh · **Status:** growing. This is the
whole plan — the rule of the magic, the wound, and who is behind the last door. Spoilers, on
purpose: read it if you want to know exactly what you're getting into.

## Why a novella

One conflict, one change. A locksmith who can open everything and has bolted her own life shut,
and the single thing she has to say out loud before the last door will open. That's a novella's
engine exactly — concentration, not sprawl: one spine, a handful of subplots all hooked to it, no
wandering. Eleven chapters, each a real scene, aimed at one accumulating effect: the sound of a
bolt finally drawn *back*.

## The universe rule, stated plainly

The Hollow Hours is our city with one soft difference: between one and two in the morning, small
kindnesses of magic leak in — never grand, always domestic, and always costing you one honest
truth, because you have to *let* it help. This book's particular leak: in the hollow hour, if Nell
opens a lock while telling it something true she has never said aloud, the door opens not onto its
own room but onto the place she most needs to reach. Once per truth. And the hollow hour keeps the
truth — once it's said, it's loose in the world; she can't take it back. That's the price: not
blood, not danger. Honesty she can't afford.

## Premise

Nell Corrigan works the two o'clock calls — the locked-out, the snapped keys, and the "closer"
jobs nobody else will touch: changing the locks on tenants being put out. She's good, fast, and
asks nothing, and she's paid off three years of quiet by keeping her own door bolted against
everyone. Then a lock she's picking in the hollow hour swings open onto a room from her past, and
the city's small magic starts offering her, again and again, the one door she has refused to stand
at: the way back to Theo, the brother she shut out and has called *gone* ever since.

## The main character (flawed, first person)

**Nell Corrigan** wants to owe no one and be owed nothing — the ledger closed, the door bolted,
the silence she calls peace. What she *needs* is to let a door be opened for her, the single lock
she's never been able to pick. Her flaw is that she mistakes a bolt for safety: she'll open any
door in the city for a stranger and turn the lock on anyone who gets close. Three years ago her
brother came to her door owing the wrong people, and she turned the bolt and has told the version
where he *left* ever since. The magic doesn't fix that. It just keeps handing her the truth and
waiting for her to say it.

## The cast (each hooked to the one conflict)

- **Theo Corrigan** — the brother, the door. Not a ghost, not a lesson: a person surviving on the
  far side of the city at the same small hours, believing she never wanted him back.
- **Mar** — the dispatcher who runs the night-call service, gruff and warm, who covered for Nell
  the night everything broke and has held her "debt" ever since. She's the one who finally says the
  bolt is on the *inside*.
- **Sully (Mr. Ilesanmi)** — a recurring hollow-hour client, an old man locked out of his own flat
  night after night because grief has him hiding his own keys. A mirror: what a lifetime of
  refusing help looks like from the outside.
- **Dani** — a young mother whose lock Nell is hired to change (an eviction). She's doing exactly
  what Nell does: bolting the world out to survive. She's the job Nell can't do — the moral hinge.

## The arc (want vs. need)

Nell **wants** the ledger closed. She **needs** to be let in. The book turns her from the woman who
bolts doors against the people she loves into the one who leaves a door open on purpose. Locking-out
vs. letting-in, run the whole way through: the trade, the magic, the wound, and the ending all say
the same thing in different keys.

## Chapters (the plan)

1. **The Two O'Clock Call.** Establish Nell, the night trade, the wry closed-off voice — through a
   routine lock-out where we see she can open anything and asks nothing, her own life bolted tight.
   A first faint wrongness in the hollow hour (a lock opens too easily, onto the wrong room; she
   dismisses it). Ends when Mar rings with a *closer* job, and we learn Nell owes her. *(written)*
2. **The Closer.** She does the eviction lock-change and tells herself it's only locks — the ugly
   economics, her no-questions rule. The theme's first hard rhyme: a door shut on someone who
   needed it open. Cutting a key at 1 a.m., she mutters something true by accident and a door
   opens, for one impossible second, onto a place from her past. She slams it. The rule, glimpsed.
3. **Sully's Keys.** The recurring client: the old man locked out nightly because he hides his own
   keys from himself. Warmth and comedy and ache. He tells her, plainly, the difference between
   *losing* a key and *hiding* one — and doesn't know he's describing her.
4. **The Bolt.** The wound named. Who Theo is; the night three years ago he came owing the wrong
   people and she turned the lock; the story she's told since. The hollow hour tests her: a door
   offers him. She refuses — won't pay the truth.
5. **What It Costs.** Nell deliberately learns the mechanism — hollow hour, plus one true unsaid
   thing, equals the door you need; and the truth, once said, is spent and loose. She spends a
   small one to reach a good old memory, and feels how saying it aloud changes her. Tempting and
   frightening in equal measure.
6. **The Job She Can't Do.** The closer job that breaks her: the tenant to be locked out is Dani
   and her kid, bolting the world out exactly the way Nell does. Nell can't turn the bolt. First
   refusal of the ugly work — and a reckoning with Mar and the debt begins.
7. **What Mar Holds.** The debt, revealed: Mar covered for Nell the night Theo left, and the job
   has been a lifeline dressed as an obligation. Gruff and unsparing, Mar tells her the thing no one
   has: the lock on Nell's own life is on the *inside*, and no one else can throw it back.
8. **The Long Refusal.** Nell almost runs — from the job, the city, all of it (who a person becomes
   when they run). The low point. Then Sully isn't at his door one night, and the fear of a door
   that stays shut *forever* detonates: she sees that refusing the truth is choosing the locked
   door for good.
9. **Telling the Lock.** The hollow hour. Nell goes to the truest door and finally says the bolted
   thing — *I locked him out. He didn't leave. I turned the bolt, and I've called it his leaving
   for three years.* She pays the whole truth. The door opens.
10. **The Door to Theo.** It opens onto where Theo actually is now — an all-night place across the
    city. The reunion: wary, tentative, real; not a fix, a first honest hour. His side of it — alive,
    surviving, sure she never wanted him back. Earned and restrained; the heartbreak lands sideways,
    and so does the mercy.
11. **The Unlocked Door.** Resolution. Nell squares Dani's eviction rather than doing it, and settles
    things with Mar; she's done being the closer. Last image: her own door, 1 a.m., kettle on, the
    bolt drawn back and left back — an invitation instead of a wall. A door ajar, on purpose.

## Ending

Not a rescue and not a tidy bow. Nell finds Theo and it's a beginning, not a cure — a second hour
earned, not promised. She stops locking people out for money and for safety both, and the final
beat is the smallest, truest thing she's done in three years: she leaves her own door unlocked and
the kettle on, and waits, which is the one lock she could never pick from the inside. Warm, wry,
and earned — heartbreak drawn sideways, the getaway slowed for once on purpose.

## Content note

Estrangement between siblings, a brother's past trouble and debt (kept off-page and un-glamorised),
an eviction, and grief held at arm's length. The Hollow Hours is a kind universe and this stays
inside that kindness: it goes tender and aching, never bleak, and nothing is graphic. A standalone —
this story is meant simply to end.
