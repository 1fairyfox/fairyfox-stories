**Form:** novelette (~14,000-word cap) · **Genre:** fantasy / fable · **POV:** first person, past
tense · **Universe:** the Salt Road · **Author:** Amara Okri · **Status:** growing. The whole tale,
spoiled on purpose — including the lie at the bottom of it.

## Why a novelette (as a fable)

A fable is a machine of threes, and this one runs on three tries at an impossible catch — mastery,
trickery, truth. Seven chapters give the tries room to cost something between them: a real road, a
real vise, and a finale that has to land a catch, a bargain's hook, and a reckoning in one motion.
One spine, no B-plot; long enough for the lie to have forty years of weight, short enough to read in
an evening.

## Premise

**Basri Yel** is the most famous wind-catcher on the Salt Road: ninety-nine winds caught, named, and
bottled, and a legend polished smooth over forty years of telling. The **Emir Duqal** — a rich man
who hates weather and lives in a windless white house — has quietly bought every one of those bottles,
and now offers the commission of the age: catch the hundredth. Not any wind — **the Unbottled**, the
one wind no catcher has ever taken, the wind towns curse as the *Liar's Wind* because of what it does:
**it opens shut things.** Locked doors, sealed letters, held coats, kept secrets. To bottle a wind you
must speak its true name into the glass — and the Unbottled answers only to a name that is different
for every catcher: **the thing you will not say.** Basri's name for it is forty years old, and it is
not a wind's name at all. It is what really happened the morning of his famous First Catch, and who
the catch truly belonged to.

## The arc (want vs. need)

Basri **wants** the hundredth catch — the legend sealed, the name *Basri Yel, who caught a hundred
winds* cut somewhere weather can't wear it off. He **needs** to say the true thing he has built the
legend on top of: that the First Catch — the Dawn Wind, the bottle that made his name — was his elder
sister **Nuru's** catch, and that when the sand came down on them he ran, and lived, and let the towns
believe the catch was his and she was lost before it. The wind he is hunting is the one thing on the
Road that cannot be caught by the man he has spent forty years pretending to be. The tale teaches by
opening him — gently, mischievously, and in public.

## The circle

- **Basri Yel** — the narrator. A master showman of a craftsman, vain as a rooster, genuinely
  skilled — and hollow exactly where the legend is. His flaw is the polished lie; it costs him his
  apprentice-of-sorts' trust, nearly costs the winds themselves, and finally costs him the legend.
- **Noor** — eleven, the Emir's "ear," sent along to verify the catch. A foundling raised inside the
  House of Still Air among the vaulted bottles; she has never once stood in weather, and knows every
  captive wind by the sound it makes against glass at night. Truthful the way only a child raised by
  glass can be. The foil: she has nothing to protect, and the wind likes her.
- **Emir Duqal** — the buyer. Not a villain with a laugh: a grieving man. A storm took his son's ship,
  so he buys winds to still them, one by one, until no storm can ever again take anyone's son. He
  believes he is mercy itself. His way of catching — iron, tar, and nets that don't need a name — kills
  a wind into dead air.
- **Nuru Yel** — forty years gone. The better catcher, the one who taught Basri everything except how
  to tell the truth. Present the whole book the way a missing person is: in a cracked bottle-stopper
  Basri has carried since the storm, and in what the Unbottled keeps airing.

## The tale (seven chapters)

1. **The Ninety-Ninth.** Open mid-catch: Basri takes an ordinary little wind before a paying crowd —
   the craft, the patter, the fame, all shown working. Then the Emir's man, the offer, and the vise it
   comes in: the Emir owns the ninety-nine already. Catch the Unbottled and the collection is legend
   forever; refuse and it stays sealed in the white house, and Basri's name seals with it. He takes
   the road — for the legend — with Noor sent along to verify. *(written)*
2. **The Ear of the House.** The road east, where the Liar's Wind runs its seasonal circuit. Basri
   teaches his three laws of the trade in showman's patter — including the catcher's old proving law:
   *a delivered bottle is uncorked once at the buyer's threshold, to show the wind is live.* Noor's
   uncanny ear introduced; and the first town the wind has just left — every shut thing in it opened,
   secrets aired in the square, one marriage broken and one grave injustice aired that badly needed
   airing. The wind's doubleness: mischief and judgment in one weather.
3. **The First Try.** By mastery. The perfect stand, the perfect glass, forty years of technique, the
   grandest name Basri knows spoken into the bottle's mouth. The wind considers him — and opens his
   coat instead, taking nothing but a cracked bottle-stopper from his breast pocket. Failure with
   teeth: the wind has his scent now, and it stops running its circuit and starts running **his** —
   ahead of him, toward the towns of his own young years.
4. **The Second Try.** By trickery. Basri writes a confession that is *almost* true — Nuru died in the
   storm, he took the bottle after — rehearses it like an act, and performs it to the wind with the
   bottle ready. The wind flips the pages out of his hands mid-performance and posts one page each on
   the doors of the next three towns. Noor, who heard the false note before the wind did, stops
   trusting him — and quietly writes to the Emir that the catch is failing. The reply comes by fast
   rider: the Emir is coming with his vault-wagons and iron nets, to take the wind the way that needs
   no name.
5. **The Still Emir.** The breath, then the vise. The Emir arrives with his nets — and with Basri's
   own First Catch, the Dawn Wind, as a goad: catch the Unbottled by the week's end, or he practices
   the netting on the bottle that made Basri's name. And the comfortable way out opens: walk away.
   He is paid either way; the legend stays whole; nets are not his doing. He almost takes it — until
   Noor tells him, plainly, what she is: a child who has spent her whole life listening to caged
   weather knock against glass, and has never once felt wind on her face. He walks anyway. That is
   the low point: the master, leaving, with his legend intact and nothing else.
6. **The Milestone.** Alone on the road out, the wind comes for him — no bottle, no audience — and
   does what it does: opens. Not his coat this time. A milestone he has passed a hundred times,
   scoured clean of forty years of dust, with two names cut in it from the years when he and Nuru
   walked this road young. The truth comes out of him at last — first to himself, then out loud, to
   Noor, plain and unperformed: the storm, the running, the taking of the catch and the name. And with
   it, the wind's grammar, understood: its name, for him, is the sentence he has just said — and
   saying it to the wind means saying it *to everyone*, because this is the wind that opens shut
   things, and a truth given to it does not stay put. The catch is now possible, and it costs the
   legend. That is the door the finale walks through.
7. **The Hundredth Wind.** The finale, full size. Before the Emir's nets close the valley, Basri
   stands up in front of everyone — the Emir, the netmen, Noor, the towns — and calls the wind by its
   name: the whole truth, full voice, the confession as the catch. The Unbottled comes to the glass
   like a bird to a known hand. Then the hook in the bargain, sprung fair: the commission said
   *bottled and delivered*, and the old proving law says a delivered bottle is opened once at the
   buyer's threshold. At the heart of the House of Still Air, the proving cork comes out — and the
   wind that opens shut things does exactly, precisely that. Every stopper in the vault. Ninety-nine
   winds and the Dawn Wind go free through the white house at once, and the Emir gets what he asked
   for to the letter and nothing that he wanted — except the last thing the freed weather carries out
   to him, which is not a punishment, and lets him open his fists at last. Basri ends plain: the
   legend gone, the First Catch credited where it always belonged, the trade taught true to Noor —
   who stands in weather for the first time in her life — and nothing kept but a cracked stopper and
   his own right name. The moral, sideways, the way the Road likes them: **a name is a bottle; mind
   what you keep in it.**

## Ending

Basri wins the only catch that ever mattered by giving up the thing the catch was for. The wind is
caught fairly, delivered honestly, and — by the trade's own oldest law — freed along with every wind
his forty years put in glass. The Emir is not defeated; he is *answered*, and lets the weather go.
Nuru cannot be given back her life, so Basri gives her back her catch: the towns now tell the First
Catch with her name in it. He is poorer, plainer, unfamous, and finally the size of the truth — and
the tale sends you off warmer than it found you, as the Salt Road always does.

## Content note

A bright desert fable with real weight under it: an old betrayal (a companion abandoned in a
sandstorm forty years ago, believed lost — told plainly, never depicted), a grieving man who cages
winds, and a public reckoning with a long lie. Mischief, adventure, bargains with hooks, and a sly
sweet ending. Nothing graphic, nothing gory, no despair — dark in the honest places, and bright the
whole way through.
