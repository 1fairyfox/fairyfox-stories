**Form:** short story (~6,800-word cap) · **Genre:** fantasy / fable · **POV:** first person, past
tense · **Universe:** the Salt Road · **Author-persona:** Yusra Adem (spare, wry, ledger-eyed fable) ·
**Sequel to:** *The Girl Who Sold the Wind* (a new tale in her realm, not a continuation — reads
completely on its own) · **Status:** growing (1 / 3 written). The whole tale, moral and forfeit
included, is below.

## Why a short story (as a fable)

The first Zari tale was a chase — a novelette with a whole desert to cross. This one is the opposite
shape: **one situation, one turn, one impression left behind.** A girl who finally has the thing she
always wanted is asked to make it safe forever, and does — and then must give it away to stay the person
she became. That is a single effect, and a short story is the machine for a single effect. Three brisk
beats, no subplot, a small cast, and a moral closed quietly at the end like a ledger.

## Premise

Three years after she spent her fortune to send an exiled wind home, Zari has what she wanted at the
start of everything: the striped stall of the Ninth Waystation, left to her by the old keeper who had no
heir and a soft spot for a girl who'd once done a costly right thing. She deals honestly now. She is
proud of it — quietly, and then not so quietly. And she is *afraid*, all the time, of losing the one
good thing she finally holds.

Into that fear walks **Hadi Sarraf**, the wind-broker of the still city of Sabr, who buys winds and
never sells them on, until the sky above his city hangs flat and windless and safe. He offers Zari a
**standing order**: every wind she stocks, at triple price, forever — on the single condition that she
sell to no one else. It is a fair contract. There is no cheating in it anywhere. Zari, who has taught
herself that honest is the same as good, signs it, and with it a small forfeit clause written plainly and
read aloud: *sell one bottle to another, and the whole stall reverts to Sabr.* A stall that can never
fail again, in exchange for a road that can no longer stop at her door. She tells herself that is
prudence.

## The arc (want vs. need)

Zari **wants** to keep what she has — the stall, the safety, the good name. She **needs** to learn that
the reward she won in the first tale has quietly become a cage of fear, and that *enough* is not a number
**or a possession**: a wind you keep is a wind you have killed, and a road only stays a road while things
still cross it. Book one taught her not to *sell* wrongly. This one teaches her not to *keep*.

## The antagonist

Hadi is no cartoon. A storm-wind took his caravan and his people in a single afternoon when he was young,
and he has spent his life buying the weather up where it can never take anyone again. He offers Zari not
a trick but a genuine mercy as he understands it — the end of risk. He is her own first-tale lesson (*a
wind in the wrong hands is a catastrophe*) walked one step too far: *so let me take them all, where no
hand can misuse them.* He is what caution becomes when it stops being brave.

## The tale (three chapters)

1. **The Standing Order.** The Ninth Waystation, now Zari's; the fear that lives under the good fortune;
   Hadi Sarraf arrives, makes his fair and reasonable case, and Zari signs — reads the forfeit clause
   twice, finds it square, and talks herself past the one small wrongness she feels. *(written)*
2. **The Sea-Wind.** A quiet day, and then **Naima** — eleven, come a hundred days across the waste with
   the exact fare for the one bottle of **sea-wind**, so her dying grandfather (a fisherman, once) can
   smell the sea one last time. That bottle, like every bottle, is now promised to a man who will never
   open it. Zari turns the girl away, correctly, by contract — and it costs Zari *nothing*, and she
   cannot stop seeing it. (The complicity beat: she has every fact, the reward is real, and she prefers
   the safe shelf anyway.)
3. **The Whole Stall for One Bottle.** Zari breaks the binding bargain — not by out-tricking Hadi, but by
   paying its written forfeit herself. She sells Naima the sea-wind for a single coin and hands the Ninth
   Waystation to Sabr. The grandfather gets his sea. And the winds will not keep for a man who cages
   them: Sabr's shelves stand full and silent and worthless, while Zari walks out onto the open road with
   nothing but the road itself. The moral, sideways, in Adem's ledger voice. Finale costed at ~1.5× a
   middle beat — a fable owes the turn *and* the close.

## Ending

Zari wins the way she won the first time — not by cleverness but by care — and it costs her more this
time, because this time she gives up the reward the first tale earned her. She loses the stall and keeps
herself. Hadi keeps his stillness and learns it was never safety, only a full shelf of dead air. Bright,
wry, warm, morally satisfying without a word of sermon: *a fair price is not the same as a right one, and
some things you must not keep even when you have paid for them squarely.*

## Content note

A bright Salt Road fable with a hard clean edge — a grief-driven hoarder, a dying man's last wish (he is
not shown dying; the wish is gentle), and a bargain that costs our heroine the thing she loves. Mischief,
warmth, and a hard sweet lesson. Nothing graphic, nothing bleak.
