This is the whole plan, endings and all. It exists so you can choose to know exactly what
you're walking into. If you'd rather be surprised, close this and go read.

## Why a novelette

One conflict, one transformation, room to breathe but no room to waste — Maren's reckoning with
a single returned object doesn't need a novel's subplots, and it's too deep for a short story's
single turn. Seven chapters, each a scene with its own small arc, climbing one hill.

## Premise

Maren Voss keeps the returns ledger in Cape Herring: the record of everything the sea gives back.
Her brother Emil drowned twenty years ago after an argument she started; the sea never returned
him, or anything of his, and she has read that silence as a verdict — and kept the ledger, half
as penance, half as a demand. This winter the tide returns Emil's knife, unrusted. The book is
what she does with it.

## The arc (want vs. need)

Maren **wants** the sea to give Emil back. She **needs** to let something go unrecorded and
survive it. The knife forces the gap open: to file it is to keep hiding; to claim it is to grieve.

## Chapters (the plan)

1. **The Ledger.** Establish Maren, the ledger-room, the town, and the rule of the coast — through
   an ordinary morning of returns. Ends when a fisherman lays a small oilskin bundle on her desk
   and won't say where he found it. *(written)*
2. **What's Inside.** She opens it: Emil's clasp-knife, the initials she scratched into the handle
   at nine, not a fleck of rust. She does the unthinkable — files it, coldly — and can't sleep.
3. **The Argument.** Flashback woven with the present: the afternoon Emil died, the thing she said,
   the breakwater. We learn the ledger began the year after.
4. **The Claimants.** Word spreads. Others come — a mother whose son is still at sea, the old keeper
   before her — each wanting Maren to tell them what a return *means*. She has always refused to say.
   Her refusal starts to crack.
5. **The Breakwater.** Maren goes to where he drowned, at the tide she's avoided for twenty years,
   carrying the knife. A near-repeat of the old scene, alone. She almost throws it back.
6. **The Return.** The turn: she understands the sea didn't give Emil *back* — it gave her the
   argument back, to finish. She says, aloud, the apology she never made. Chooses not to file the knife.
7. **Unrecorded.** Coda. She leaves one line of the ledger blank on purpose — the first thing in
   nineteen years she lets go unaccounted. The town's next return arrives; she records it, lighter.
   Whether she keeps the knife or gives it to the sea is left to land as her choice, not a rule.

## Ending

No supernatural reunion. Emil does not come back; the "return" is Maren's, not his. The emotional
resolution is her letting the ledger be incomplete — grief finally allowed to be a thing that
doesn't balance. Earned, quiet, and traceable to her own flaw.

## Content note

Grief, drowning, and the loss of a sibling. Sad and quiet rather than graphic. No sequel is
planned; this is a standalone.
